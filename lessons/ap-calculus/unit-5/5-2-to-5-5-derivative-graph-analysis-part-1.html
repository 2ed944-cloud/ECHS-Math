PART 1

Extreme values and the closed-interval method

A function can have local extrema, absolute extrema, both, or neither. The location depends on the domain, continuity, endpoints, and critical numbers.
  • Absolute maximum: f(c)≥f(x)f(c) \ge f(x) for every x in the domain.
  • Absolute minimum: f(c)≤f(x)f(c) \le f(x) for every x in the domain.
  • Local maximum/minimum: compare only with nearby x-values.
  • Endpoints can be absolute extrema. Interior local extrema must occur at critical numbers.
THEOREM

Extreme Value Theorem

If f is continuous on a closed interval [a,b][a,b], then f attains both an absolute maximum and an absolute minimum on [a,b][a,b].
Important
The theorem guarantees existence only. It does not say the extrema occur at endpoints, and it does not say the extrema are unique.
  • Closed interval is required.
  • Continuity on the whole interval is required.
  • Open intervals, holes, jumps, and asymptotes may prevent extrema from being attained.
PROCEDURE

Closed-interval method

To find absolute extrema of a continuous function on [a,b][a,b], compare all candidates.
  1. Find every critical number inside (a,b)(a,b).
  2. Evaluate f at each interior critical number.
  3. Evaluate f at a and b.
  4. The largest value is the absolute maximum. The smallest value is the absolute minimum.

COMMON ERRORS

Inflection point traps

Solving f″(x)=0f''(x)=0 or finding where f″f'' is undefined gives only candidates.
  • Check that f is continuous at the x-value.
  • Check that concavity actually changes.
  • A horizontal tangent can be an inflection point, but it does not have to be.
  • A vertical tangent can be an inflection point, but it does not have to be.

Curve sketching workflow

  1. Domain and intercepts.
  2. Symmetry, if useful.
  3. Asymptotes and end behavior.
  4. Critical numbers and f′f' sign chart.

  6. f′′f'' sign chart and inflection points.
  7. Draw a graph consistent with all information.

Final checklist

  • State the domain first.
  • List critical numbers correctly: f′(c)=0f'(c)=0 or f′(c) is undefined while f(c) existsf'(c)\text{ is undefined while }f(c)\text{ exists}.
  • For absolute extrema on a closed interval, compare endpoints and critical values.
  • Use f′f' sign changes to justify local extrema.
  • Use f″f'' sign changes to justify inflection points.
  • Use the Second Derivative Test only when f′(c)=0f'(c)=0 and f″(c)f''(c) gives a definite sign.
  • For curve sketching, connect ff, f′f', and f″f'' consistently.
