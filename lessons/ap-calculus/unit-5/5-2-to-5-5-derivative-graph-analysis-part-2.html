QUESTION 24 · Challenge

An Impossible Critical-Point Pattern

Can a differentiable function with continuous derivative have exactly three critical numbers −2,1,3, where −2 and 3 are local minima and 1 is a horizontal inflection point? Give a rigorous sign argument.
Open working space

Use this space for a sign chart, comparison table, or justification.

Show complete solution
A local minimum at −2 requires f′ to change from negative to positive. A horizontal inflection at 1 does not change the sign of f′, so f′ remains positive immediately to the right of 1. A local minimum at 3 would require f′ to be negative immediately to the left of 3. By the intermediate-value property of derivatives, f′ would have to be zero somewhere in (1,3), creating a fourth critical number. Therefore the pattern is impossible.
QUESTION 25 · Challenge

Alternation of Simple Critical Points

Suppose f′ is continuous and has exactly three simple zeros a<b<c. Prove that, if all three produce extrema, the extrema must alternate in type.
Open working space

Use this space for a sign chart, comparison table, or justification.

Show complete solution
At a simple zero, the continuous derivative changes sign. Once the sign changes at a, the sign on (a,b) is fixed because no other zero exists there. It must change again at b, and again at c. Consequently the sign changes alternate +→−, −→+, +→−, or the reverse. Hence the extrema alternate maximum–minimum–maximum or minimum–maximum–minimum.
