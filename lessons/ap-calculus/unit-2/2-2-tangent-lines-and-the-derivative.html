2.2 Tangent Lines and the Derivative

Derivatives as limits of difference quotients, tangent-line slopes, and tangent-line equations.

ECHS Mathematics • Mohammad Abu Ghuwaleh
\[f'(x)=\lim_{h\to0}\frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h}\]

At a point, this gives the instantaneous rate of change and the slope of the tangent line.

Warm-up

For \(f(x)=x^2-3\), compute the average rates of change from \(x=2\) to \(x=3\), \(x=2.5\), \(x=2.1\), and \(x=2.01\). What number do the slopes approach?

From secant slope to tangent slope

Secant slope

\[m_{sec}=\frac{f(a+h)-f(a)}{(a+h)-a}=\frac{f(a+h)-f(a)}{h}\]

Tangent slope

\[m_{tan}=\lim_{h\to0}\frac{f(a+h)-f(a)}{h}\]

The second point moves toward the point of tangency.

AP Calculus AB

Definition of the derivative

Derivative function

\[f'(x)=\lim_{h\to0}\frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h}\]

The limit must exist. The domain of \(f'\) may be smaller than the domain of \(f\).

At a point

\[f'(a)=\lim_{h\to0}\frac{f(a+h)-f(a)}{h}\]

Alternate form

\[f'(a)=\lim_{x\to a}\frac{f(x)-f(a)}{x-a}\]

AP Calculus AB

Worked example: derivative by the limit process

Example 1

Use the limit definition to find the derivative of \(f(x)=2x^2-3x\).

\[\begin{aligned}f'(x)&=\lim_{h\to0}\frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h}\\&=\lim_{h\to0}\frac{2(x+h)^2-3(x+h)-(2x^2-3x)}{h}\\&=\lim_{h\to0}\frac{4xh+2h^2-3h}{h}\\&=\lim_{h\to0}(4x+2h-3)=4x-3.\end{aligned}\]

AP Calculus AB

Alternate form at a point

Point formula

\[f'(a)=\lim_{x\to a}\frac{f(x)-f(a)}{x-a}\]

Use when

You only need the derivative at one value \(x=a\), or the expression is already written with \(x-a\) in the denominator.

Example 2

Use the alternate form to find \(f'(1)\) for \(f(x)=3x+1\).

\(f(1)=4\), so \(f'(1)=\displaystyle\lim_{x\to1}\frac{3x+1-4}{x-1}=\lim_{x\to1}3=3\).

AP Calculus AB

Two formulas, one derivative value

Example 3

Find the slope of the tangent line to \(f(x)=\dfrac{2}{x}\) at \(x=3\) using both forms.

Using either form gives \(f'(3)=-\dfrac{2}{9}\). The tangent slope is \(-\dfrac{2}{9}\).

At \(x=3\), the point is \(\left(3,\dfrac{2}{3}\right)\).

AP Calculus AB

Equation of the tangent line

\[y-f(a)=f'(a)(x-a)\]
  1. Find the point: \((a,f(a))\).
  2. Find the slope: \(m=f'(a)\).
  3. Substitute into point-slope form.

Quick check

If \(f(4)=-2\) and \(f'(4)=7\), write the tangent line at \(x=4\).

\(y+2=7(x-4)\).
AP Calculus AB

Guided example: tangent line

Example 4

Write the equation of the tangent line to \(f(x)=x^2-3\) at \(x=2\).

\(f(2)=1\), \(f'(x)=2x\), so \(f'(2)=4\). Tangent line: \(y-1=4(x-2)\).

AP Calculus AB

Normal line

Normal slope

\[m_{normal}=-\frac{1}{f'(a)}\quad\text{if }f'(a)\ne0\]

Normal line

\[y-f(a)=-\frac{1}{f'(a)}(x-a)\]

Quick check

If \(f(5)=8\) and \(f'(5)=-4\), write the normal line at \(x=5\).

Normal slope \(=\dfrac{1}{4}\). Line: \(y-8=\dfrac14(x-5)\).
AP Calculus AB

Tangent and normal from a derivative

Example 5

For \(f(x)=7-3x-x^2\), find the tangent and normal lines at \(x=1\).

\(f'(x)=-3-2x\), so \(f'(1)=-5\), and \(f(1)=3\). Tangent: \(y-3=-5(x-1)\). Normal: \(y-3=\dfrac15(x-1)\).

AP Calculus AB

Radical example: tangent and normal

Example 6

For \(g(x)=\sqrt{2-x}\), write the tangent and normal lines at \(x=-7\).

\(g(-7)=3\). Using the limit process, \(g'(x)=-\dfrac{1}{2\sqrt{2-x}}\), so \(g'(-7)=-\dfrac16\). Tangent: \(y-3=-\dfrac16(x+7)\). Normal: \(y-3=6(x+7)\).

Horizontal tangents

Example 7

Find the points where \(f(x)=x^3-3x\) has a horizontal tangent.

\(f'(x)=3x^2-3\). Set \(3x^2-3=0\), so \(x=\pm1\). Points: \((-1,2)\) and \((1,-2)\).

AP Calculus AB

Prescribed tangent slope

Example 8

Find all points where the slope of the tangent line to \(y=x^3+3x+1\) equals \(5\).

\(y'=3x^2+3\). Solve \(3x^2+3=5\), so \(x=\pm\sqrt{\frac23}\). The points are \(\left(\sqrt{\frac23},\left(\sqrt{\frac23}\right)^3+3\sqrt{\frac23}+1\right)\) and \(\left(-\sqrt{\frac23},-\left(\sqrt{\frac23}\right)^3-3\sqrt{\frac23}+1\right)\).

Try

Find the value of \(c\) so that the tangent line to \(y=x^2+1\) at \(x=c\) is parallel to \(y=x\).

Parallel slope is \(1\). Since \(y'=2x\), \(2c=1\), so \(c=\dfrac12\).
AP Calculus AB

Tangents at intercepts

Example 9

Find the equation of the tangent line to \(f(x)=x^2-4x\) at each \(x\)-intercept.

Intercepts from \(x^2-4x=x(x-4)=0\): \(x=0,4\). Since \(f'(x)=2x-4\), slopes are \(-4\) and \(4\). Tangents: at \((0,0)\), \(y=-4x\); at \((4,0)\), \(y=4(x-4)\).

Try

Find the tangent line to \(y=x^2+2x\) at \(x=1\), then find its \(x\)- and \(y\)-intercepts.

\(y'=2x+2\), so slope at \(1\) is \(4\); point is \((1,3)\). Tangent: \(y-3=4(x-1)\Rightarrow y=4x-1\). Intercepts: \(y\)-intercept \((0,-1)\), \(x\)-intercept \(\left(\dfrac14,0\right)\).

Graph check: derivative or no derivative?

Question

Does the function have a derivative at \(x=0\)? Explain using slopes.

No. The left slope is \(1\), and the right slope is \(-2\). The one-sided tangent slopes do not agree.

Extension

Could the function still be continuous at \(x=0\)?

Yes. A function can be continuous but not differentiable at a corner.

Challenge 1: average velocity shortcut

33

For a quadratic position function \(s(t)=at^2+bt+c\), show that the average velocity between \(t=r\) and \(t=s\) equals the average of the velocities at \(t=r\) and \(t=s\).

Average velocity: \(\dfrac{s(s)-s(r)}{s-r}=a(s+r)+b\). Velocities: \(v(r)=2ar+b\), \(v(s)=2as+b\). Their average is \(\dfrac{(2ar+b)+(2as+b)}{2}=a(r+s)+b\).

38

Show that

\[\lim_{h\to0}\frac{f(a+h)-f(a)}{h}=\lim_{x\to a}\frac{f(x)-f(a)}{x-a}\]

Hint: let \(h=x-a\).

Geometric reasoning

Parallel, perpendicular, and angle conditions

1. Parallel tangent

Find the point(s) where the tangent to \(f(x)=x^2-4x+1\) is parallel to the line \(3x-2y=5\). Then write the tangent line.

The line has slope \(\frac32\). Since \(f'(x)=2x-4\), solve \(2x-4=\frac32\), so \(x=\frac{11}{4}\).

\(f\!\left(\frac{11}{4}\right)=-\frac{39}{16}\).

Tangent: \(y+\frac{39}{16}=\frac32\left(x-\frac{11}{4}\right)\).

2. Given angle of inclination

The tangent line to \(f(x)=x^2-4x+1\) makes an angle of \(135^\circ\) with the positive \(x\)-axis. Find the point of tangency and the tangent line.

Slope \(m=\tan(135^\circ)=-1\). Solve \(2x-4=-1\), so \(x=\frac32\).

\(f\!\left(\frac32\right)=-\frac{11}{4}\). Tangent: \(y+\frac{11}{4}=-(x-\frac32)\).

3. Normal-line condition

Find the point(s) on \(y=x^3-3x\) where the normal line has slope \(-\frac12\).

If the normal slope is \(-\frac12\), the tangent slope is \(2\). Since \(f'(x)=3x^2-3\), solve \(3x^2-3=2\).

\(x=\pm\sqrt{\frac53}\). The points are \(\left(\sqrt{\frac53},\,\left(\sqrt{\frac53}\right)^3-3\sqrt{\frac53}\right)\) and \(\left(-\sqrt{\frac53},\,-\left(\sqrt{\frac53}\right)^3+3\sqrt{\frac53}\right)\).

Tricky tangent conditions

Tangent line through a point not on the curve

For a tangent to \(y=x^2\) at \(x=c\), the point of tangency is \((c,c^2)\) and the tangent slope is \(2c\).

\[y-c^2=2c(x-c)\]

Problem

Find all tangent lines to \(y=x^2\) that pass through \((2,-3)\).

Substitute \((2,-3)\) into \(y-c^2=2c(x-c)\):

\(-3-c^2=2c(2-c)\Rightarrow c^2-4c-3=0\).

\(c=2\pm\sqrt7\). The tangent lines are

\(y-(2+\sqrt7)^2=2(2+\sqrt7)\bigl(x-(2+\sqrt7)\bigr)\)

and

\(y-(2-\sqrt7)^2=2(2-\sqrt7)\bigl(x-(2-\sqrt7)\bigr)\).

Challenge

Tangent line meets the curve again

1

Let \(f(x)=x^3+3x+1\). Find the tangent line at \(x=1\). Then determine whether this tangent line intersects the graph of \(f\) at any other point.

\(f(1)=5\) and \(f'(1)=6\). Tangent: \(y-5=6(x-1)\), or \(y=6x-1\).

Intersect with the curve:

\(x^3+3x+1=6x-1\Rightarrow x^3-3x+2=0\Rightarrow (x-1)^2(x+2)=0\).

Besides \(x=1\), the line intersects again at \(x=-2\), where \(y=-13\).

2

Explain why a tangent line can intersect a curve more than once without contradicting the meaning of tangent line.

A tangent line models the curve locally at the point of tangency by matching the point and slope. It can cross or touch the curve again elsewhere.

Geometric applications

Tangent and normal with the coordinate axes

1. Tangent-axis triangle

For \(f(x)=x^2+7\), find the tangent line at \(x=1\). Then find the area of the triangle formed by this tangent line and the coordinate axes.

At \(x=1\), \(f(1)=8\) and \(f'(1)=2\). Tangent: \(y-8=2(x-1)\), so \(y=2x+6\).

Intercepts: \((-3,0)\), \((0,6)\). Area \(=\frac12(3)(6)=9\).

2. Normal-axis triangle

For the same function, find the normal line at \(x=1\). Then find the area of the triangle formed by the normal line and the coordinate axes.

Normal slope \(=-\frac12\). Normal: \(y-8=-\frac12(x-1)\), so \(y=-\frac12x+\frac{17}{2}\).

Parameter challenge

Build the function from the tangent or normal line

1

Find \(k\) and \(b\) so that the tangent line to \(f(x)=x^2+kx+b\) at \(x=1\) is \(y=5x-4\).

Derivative: \(f'(x)=2x+k\). The tangent slope is \(5\), so \(2+k=5\Rightarrow k=3\).

The tangent line has value \(1\) at \(x=1\). Since \(f(1)=1+k+b\), \(1+3+b=1\Rightarrow b=-3\).

2

Find \(a\) and \(b\) so that the normal line to \(f(x)=x^2+ax+b\) at \(x=1\) is \(y=-\frac14x+\frac74\).

The normal slope is \(-\frac14\), so the tangent slope is \(4\). Since \(f'(x)=2x+a\), \(2+a=4\Rightarrow a=2\).

The normal line gives \(y=\frac32\) at \(x=1\). Thus \(f(1)=1+2+b=\frac32\), so \(b=-\frac32\).

When the derivative does not exist

Classify each point

For each function, decide whether \(f'(a)\) exists. Justify using one-sided slopes or the limit definition.

  1. \(f(x)=|x-2|\), \(a=2\)
  2. \(f(x)=|x|+x\), \(a=0\)
  3. \(f(x)=x^{2/3}\), \(a=0\)
  4. \(f(x)=\dfrac{x}{|x|}\), \(a=0\)
  1. No. Left slope \(-1\), right slope \(1\).
  2. No. Left slope \(0\), right slope \(2\).
  3. No finite derivative. The slopes become unbounded near \(0\).
  4. No. The function is not even defined at \(0\), so it cannot be differentiable there.

Error analysis

A student says: “\(|x-2|\) has a tangent line at \(x=2\) because the graph is continuous there.” Explain the error and write a correct conclusion.

Continuity is necessary for differentiability, but it is not sufficient. At \(x=2\), the graph has a sharp corner, so the left and right tangent slopes are different. Therefore \(f'(2)\) does not exist.

Lesson summary

Derivative

\[f'(x)=\lim_{h\to0}\frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h}\]

Tangent line

\[y-f(a)=f'(a)(x-a)\]

Alternate form

\[f'(a)=\lim_{x\to a}\frac{f(x)-f(a)}{x-a}\]

Normal line

\[y-f(a)=-\frac{1}{f'(a)}(x-a)\]

Use only when \(f'(a)\ne0\).
