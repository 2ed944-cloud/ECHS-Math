2.3 The Derivative: Graphs, Differentiability, and Continuity

Estimating derivatives, connecting graphs of functions and derivatives, and deciding where derivatives exist.

\[f'(a)=\lim_{h\to0}\frac{f(a+h)-f(a)}{h}=\lim_{x\to a}\frac{f(x)-f(a)}{x-a}\]

Focus

Use the derivative as a number, a function, a graph, and a reasoned claim about continuity and smoothness.

Opening decision

A graph has no break at \(x=2\), but it has a sharp corner there. Is the function continuous at \(x=2\)? Is it differentiable at \(x=2\)?

Continuous: yes. Differentiable: no. At a corner, the left-hand and right-hand tangent slopes are different.
Launch

Derivative as an instant rate

From secant to tangent

The average rate of change over \([a,b]\) is a secant slope.

\[\frac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a}\]

The instantaneous rate at \(a\) is the limit of nearby secant slopes.

\[f'(a)=\lim_{x\to a}\frac{f(x)-f(a)}{x-a}\]
Two Forms

Same derivative, two useful limits

Form 1

\[f'(a)=\lim_{h\to0}\frac{f(a+h)-f(a)}{h}\]

Useful when the nearby input is written as \(a+h\).

Form 2

\[f'(a)=\lim_{x\to a}\frac{f(x)-f(a)}{x-a}\]

Useful when the nearby input is written as \(x\).

Quick match

Identify the function and the point represented by

\[\lim_{x\to 2}\frac{\sqrt{3x+1}-\sqrt{7}}{x-2}\]
The function is \(f(x)=\sqrt{3x+1}\), and the derivative is being taken at \(x=2\), since \(f(2)=\sqrt7\).
Derivative Function

Derivative as a function

A new function

When the input remains \(x\), the limit gives a formula that returns a tangent slope at each allowed input.

\[f'(x)=\lim_{h\to0}\frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h}\]

Example

Use the limit definition to find \(f'(x)\) for \(f(x)=x^2-4x+5\). Then find \(f'(0)\), \(f'(2)\), and \(f'(5)\).

\(f(x+h)-(f(x))=(x+h)^2-4(x+h)+5-(x^2-4x+5)=2xh+h^2-4h\). Divide by \(h\): \(2x+h-4\). Let \(h\to0\): \(f'(x)=2x-4\). Then \(f'(0)=-4\), \(f'(2)=0\), \(f'(5)=6\).

Differentiability

Differentiability and continuity

Key theorem

If \(f\) is differentiable at \(x=c\), then \(f\) is continuous at \(x=c\).

\[\text{Differentiable at }c \Longrightarrow \text{continuous at }c\]

Converse warning

Continuity does not guarantee differentiability. A continuous graph can still have a corner, cusp, or vertical tangent.

\[\text{Continuous at }c \not\Rightarrow \text{differentiable at }c\]

True or false

  1. If \(f\) is differentiable at \(a\), then \(\lim_{x\to a}f(x)=f(a)\).
  2. If \(\lim_{x\to a}f(x)=f(a)\), then \(f'(a)\) must exist.
1) True. 2) False. Example: \(f(x)=|x|\) is continuous at \(0\), but \(f'(0)\) does not exist.

One-Sided Derivatives

Corner: one-sided derivative test

Show not differentiable

Let \(f(x)=|x-1|\). Use one-sided derivative limits at \(x=1\).

\(f(1)=0\). For \(h>0\), \(\dfrac{f(1+h)-f(1)}{h}=\dfrac{|h|}{h}=1\). For \(h<0\), \(\dfrac{|h|}{h}=-1\). Since the one-sided derivatives are unequal, \(f'(1)\) does not exist.
One-Sided Derivatives

Piecewise derivative at a transition

Example

Show that \(f(x)=\begin{cases}4,&x<2\\2x,&x\ge2\end{cases}\) is not differentiable at \(x=2\).

\(f(2)=4\). From the left, \(\dfrac{f(2+h)-f(2)}{h}=0\). From the right, \(\dfrac{2(2+h)-4}{h}=2\). Since \(0\ne2\), \(f'(2)\) does not exist.
Vertical Tangent

Vertical tangent: derivative is not finite

Use the alternate form

For \(f(x)=\sqrt[3]{x}\), examine \(\displaystyle\lim_{x\to0}\frac{f(x)-f(0)}{x-0}\).

\(\dfrac{\sqrt[3]{x}}{x}=\dfrac{1}{x^{2/3}}\to\infty\) from both sides. The tangent line is vertical at \(x=0\); the derivative does not exist as a finite real number.

2

Parameter Challenge

Make a piecewise function differentiable

1

Find all real numbers \(a\) and \(b\) such that \(f'(0)\) exists.

\[f(x)=\begin{cases}x^2+2x,&x<0\\ax+b,&x\ge0\end{cases}\]
Continuity at \(0\): left value \(0\), right value \(b\), so \(b=0\). Left derivative at \(0\) is \(2\); right derivative is \(a\). Therefore \(a=2\), \(b=0\).

2

Find \(k\) and \(m\) so that \(g\) is differentiable at \(x=1\).

\[g(x)=\begin{cases}kx^2+1,&x\le1\\\sqrt{x+3}+m,&x>1\end{cases}\]
Continuity: \(k+1=2+m\), so \(m=k-1\). Left derivative at \(1\): \(2k\). Right derivative: \(\dfrac{1}{2\sqrt{4}}=\dfrac14\). Thus \(2k=\dfrac14\), so \(k=\dfrac18\) and \(m=-\dfrac78\).

AP Style

AP-style conceptual check

1

If \(f\) is continuous on \([a,b]\) but not differentiable at some \(c\), where \(a<c<b\), which could be true?

  1. \(x=c\) is a vertical asymptote of \(f\).
  2. \(\lim_{x\to c}f(x)\ne f(c)\).
  3. The graph of \(f\) has a cusp at \(x=c\).
  4. \(f(c)\) is undefined.
C. A cusp can be continuous but not differentiable. Choices A, B, and D violate continuity.

2

If \(g\) is differentiable at \(x=c\), which must be true?

I. \(g\) is continuous at \(c\)II. \(\lim_{x\to c}g(x)\) existsIII. \(\lim_{h\to0}\dfrac{g(c+h)-g(c)}{h}\) exists

I, II, and III. Differentiability includes existence of the derivative limit and implies continuity.

Hard Limit

Derivative limit transformations

1

If \(f\) is differentiable at \(x=a\), evaluate:

\[\lim_{h\to0}\frac{f(a+ch)-f(a)}{h}\]
Multiply and divide by \(ch\): \(\dfrac{f(a+ch)-f(a)}{ch}\cdot c\to c f'(a)\).

2

If \(f\) is differentiable at \(a\ne0\), evaluate:

\[\lim_{x\to a}\frac{[f(x)]^2-[f(a)]^2}{x^2-a^2}\]
Factor: \(\dfrac{(f(x)-f(a))(f(x)+f(a))}{(x-a)(x+a)}\). The limit is \(f'(a)\cdot\dfrac{2f(a)}{2a}=\dfrac{f(a)f'(a)}{a}\).
Hard Limit

More derivative limit forms

1

If \(f(3)=5\) and \(f'(3)=-2\), evaluate:

\[\lim_{x\to3}\frac{x f(3)-3f(x)}{x-3}\]
Rewrite numerator: \(5x-3f(x)=5(x-3)+15-3f(x)=5(x-3)-3(f(x)-5)\). Divide by \(x-3\): \(5-3\dfrac{f(x)-f(3)}{x-3}\to5-3(-2)=11\).

2

Suppose \(f(0)=0\), \(f'(0)\) exists, and \(f(x)\le x\) for all \(x\). What can be concluded about \(f'(0)\)?

For \(h>0\), \(\dfrac{f(h)}{h}\le1\). For \(h<0\), division by a negative reverses the inequality, so \(\dfrac{f(h)}{h}\ge1\). If the two-sided limit exists, the only possible value is \(1\). Hence \(f'(0)=1\).
Derivative Limits

Derivative-limit traps

1

If \(f\) is differentiable at \(x=4\), \(f(4)=5\), and \(f'(4)=3\), evaluate:

\[\lim_{x\to4}\frac{f(x)-5}{x-4}\]
This is exactly the alternate form of the derivative at \(x=4\), so the value is \(f'(4)=3\).

2

If \(h\) is differentiable at \(x=1\), \(h(1)=6\), and \(h'(1)=0\), evaluate:

\[\lim_{u\to0}\frac{h(1+u)-6}{u}\]
Since \(h(1)=6\), the numerator is \(h(1+u)-h(1)\). The limit is \(h'(1)=0\).

3

If \(p(3)=2\) and \(p'(3)=-1\), evaluate:

\[\lim_{x\to3}\frac{p(x)-2}{3-x}\]
\(3-x=-(x-3)\), so the limit is \(-p'(3)=1\).

4

A function \(H\) satisfies \(H(x+y)=H(x)H(y)\), \(H(0)=1\), and

\[\lim_{u\to0}\frac{H(u)-1}{u}=3.\]

Prove \(H\) is differentiable and find \(H'(x)\).

\(\displaystyle \frac{H(x+u)-H(x)}{u}=\frac{H(x)H(u)-H(x)}{u}=H(x)\frac{H(u)-1}{u}\to3H(x)\). Therefore \(H'(x)=3H(x)\).
One-sided Derivatives

One-sided derivative limit challenges

For each problem, let \(h(3)=6\), \(h'_+(3)=4\), and \(h'_-(3)=2\).

1

Determine whether each limit exists. If it exists, find its value.

\[\lim_{x\to3}\frac{h(x)-6}{|x-3|}\qquad\text{and}\qquad \lim_{x\to3}\frac{|h(x)-6|}{x-3}\]
Use \(h(x)-6\sim h'_\pm(3)(x-3)\). For the first limit: left side \(-2\), right side \(4\), so it does not exist. For the second limit, since both one-sided slopes are positive, \(|h(x)-6|\sim |h'_\pm(3)(x-3)|\). Left side \(-2\), right side \(4\), so it does not exist.

2

Find \(a\), if it exists, so that the limit exists.

\[\lim_{x\to3}\frac{h(x)-6-a|x-3|}{x-3}\]
Right side: \(4-a\). Left side: \(2+a\). Set \(2+a=4-a\), so \(a=1\). The limit value would be \(3\).

3

Find the limit.

\[\lim_{x\to3}\frac{h(x)-6}{\sqrt{|x-3|}}\]
Since \(h(x)-6=O(x-3)\), the quotient behaves like a constant times \(\dfrac{x-3}{\sqrt{|x-3|}}\), which tends to \(0\) from both sides. The limit is \(0\).

4

Find the limit, or explain why it cannot be a two-sided finite limit.

\[\lim_{x\to3}\frac{h(x)-h(3)}{x-3+|x-3|}\]
For \(x>3\), the denominator is \(2(x-3)\), giving \(\frac{1}{2}h'_+(3)=2\). For \(x<3\), the denominator is \(0\), so the expression is not defined on the left side. The two-sided limit does not exist.
One-sided Derivatives

Build a limit from unequal one-sided slopes

For each problem, let \(h(3)=6\), \(h'_+(3)=4\), and \(h'_-(3)=2\).

5

Determine whether the following limit exists.

\[\lim_{x\to3}\frac{h(x)-6-3(x-3)}{|x-3|}\]
Right side: \(\dfrac{(4-3)(x-3)}{|x-3|}=1\). Left side: \(\dfrac{(2-3)(x-3)}{|x-3|}=1\). The limit exists and equals \(1\).

6

Find \(a\), if it exists, so that the following limit exists.

\[\lim_{x\to3}\frac{h(x)-6-a(x-3)}{|x-3|}\]
Right side: \(4-a\). Left side: \(-(2-a)=a-2\). Set \(4-a=a-2\), so \(a=3\). The common limit is \(1\).

7

If \(f\) is differentiable at \(x=1\) and

\[\lim_{h\to0}\frac{f(1+h)-3h+h^2}{h}=8,\]

find \(f(1)+f'(1)\).

Write the expression as \(\dfrac{f(1+h)-f(1)}{h}+\dfrac{f(1)}{h}-3+h\). A finite limit forces \(f(1)=0\). Then \(f'(1)-3=8\), so \(f'(1)=11\). Thus \(f(1)+f'(1)=11\).

8

If \(f\) is differentiable at \(x=1\) and

\[\lim_{h\to0}\frac{f(1+h)-f(1-h)+4h}{2h}=7,\]

find \(f'(1)\).

Since \(f(1+h)-f(1-h)=[f(1+h)-f(1)]+[f(1)-f(1-h)]\), dividing by \(2h\) gives \(\frac{f'(1)+f'(1)}{2}=f'(1)\). Hence \(f'(1)+2=7\), so \(f'(1)=5\).
Derivative Limits

Parameters inside derivative limits

9

If \(f\) is differentiable at \(x=1\) and

\[\lim_{h\to0}\frac{f(1+h)+ah}{h}=b,\]

find \(f(1)\) and \(f'(1)\) in terms of \(a\) and \(b\).

A finite limit forces \(f(1)=0\). Then the limit becomes \(f'(1)+a=b\), so \(f'(1)=b-a\).

10

If \(h(x+y)=h(x)h(y)\), \(h(0)=1\), and

\[\lim_{x\to0}\frac{h(x)-1}{x}=3,\]

prove that

\[\lim_{t\to0}\frac{h(x+t)-h(x)}{t}=3h(x).\]
Use \(h(x+t)=h(x)h(t)\). Then \(\dfrac{h(x+t)-h(x)}{t}=h(x)\dfrac{h(t)-1}{t}\to3h(x)\).
Challenge

Nested derivative limits

1

Let \(f\) be differentiable at \(x=1\), and suppose

\[\lim_{h\to0}\frac{f(1+h^2)-f(1-h^2)+h f(1+h)-h f(1-h)}{h^2}=10.\]

Find \(f'(1)\).

\(\dfrac{f(1+h^2)-f(1-h^2)}{h^2}\to 2f'(1)\). Also \(\dfrac{h(f(1+h)-f(1-h))}{h^2}=\dfrac{f(1+h)-f(1-h)}{h}\to2f'(1)\). Therefore the full limit is \(4f'(1)=10\), so \(f'(1)=\dfrac52\).

2

Let \(f\) be differentiable at \(x=1\). For real constants \(a\) and \(\lambda\), define

\[L(a,\lambda)=\lim_{h\to0}\frac{f(1+h+a|h|)+\lambda f(1-h+a|h|)-(1+\lambda)f(1)}{h}.\]

Determine all values of \(a\) and \(\lambda\) for which \(L(a,\lambda)\) is guaranteed to exist for every differentiable \(f\). Then express \(L(a,\lambda)\) in terms of \(f'(1)\).

For \(h>0\), the coefficient of \(f'(1)\) is \((1+a)+\lambda(a-1)\). For \(h<0\), it is \((1-a)+\lambda(-1-a)\). These must match for every differentiable \(f\), so \(2a(1+\lambda)=0\). Thus \(a=0\) or \(\lambda=-1\). If \(a=0\), then \(L=(1-\lambda)f'(1)\). If \(\lambda=-1\), then \(L=2f'(1)\).
Functional Equation

Proving differentiability from a local limit

1

Let \(h(x+y)=h(x)h(y)\) for all real \(x,y\). Suppose

\[h(x)=1+xL(x),\qquad \lim_{x\to0}L(x)=2.\]

Prove that \(h\) is differentiable on \(\mathbb{R}\), and find \(h'(x)\) in terms of \(h(x)\).

\(\dfrac{h(t)-1}{t}=L(t)\to2\). Then \(\dfrac{h(x+t)-h(x)}{t}=h(x)\dfrac{h(t)-1}{t}\to2h(x)\). Therefore \(h'(x)=2h(x)\).

2

Let \(h(x+y)=h(x)h(y)\) and

\[\lim_{x\to0}\frac{h(x)-1}{x}=5.\]

Prove that \(h\) is differentiable on \(\mathbb{R}\), and prove that \(h'(x)=5h(x)\).

For any fixed \(x\), \(\dfrac{h(x+t)-h(x)}{t}=h(x)\dfrac{h(t)-1}{t}\to5h(x)\). Thus \(h'(x)=5h(x)\).

3

Let \(h(x+y)=h(x)h(y)\). Suppose \(h\) is differentiable at \(0\) and \(h'(0)=k\). Prove that \(h\) is differentiable for every real \(x\), and find \(h'(x)\).

If \(h(0)=0\), then \(h(x)=h(x)h(0)=0\) for all \(x\), so \(k=0\) and \(h'(x)=kh(x)\). Otherwise \(h(0)=1\), so \(\lim_{t\to0}\dfrac{h(t)-1}{t}=k\). Then \(\dfrac{h(x+t)-h(x)}{t}=h(x)\dfrac{h(t)-1}{t}\to kh(x)\).

4

Let \(h(x+y)=h(x)h(y)\), \(h(x)=1+xL(x)\), and \(\lim_{x\to0}L(x)=1\). Prove that

\[\lim_{x\to0}\frac{h(ax)-1}{x}=a\]

for every real constant \(a\).

\(h(ax)=1+axL(ax)\), so \(\dfrac{h(ax)-1}{x}=aL(ax)\to a\).
Functional Equation

Extensions from \(h(x+y)=h(x)h(y)\)

Assume \(h(x+y)=h(x)h(y)\), \(h(x)=1+xL(x)\), and \(\lim_{x\to0}L(x)=1\), unless stated otherwise.

1

Prove that \(h(-x)=\dfrac{1}{h(x)}\) for every real \(x\), and use this to show that \(h(x)\ne0\) for all real \(x\).

From \(h(0)=h(x+(-x))=h(x)h(-x)\). Also \(h(0)=1\) from \(h(t)=1+tL(t)\) at \(t=0\). Hence \(h(x)h(-x)=1\), so \(h(x)\ne0\) and \(h(-x)=1/h(x)\).

2

Suppose instead that \(\lim_{x\to0}\dfrac{h(x)-1}{x}=4\). Prove that \(h(x)>0\) for all real \(x\), and then prove that \(h'(x)=4h(x)\).

The local limit gives \(h(t)\to1\), so for small \(t\), \(h(t)>0\). For any \(x\), write \(x=n(x/n)\) with \(n\) large enough that \(h(x/n)>0\). Then \(h(x)=h(x/n)^n>0\). The derivative proof gives \(h'(x)=4h(x)\).

3

Define \(F(x)=h(x^2)\). Prove that \(F\) is differentiable for every real \(x\), and find \(F'(x)\) in terms of \(x\) and \(h(x^2)\).

Use \(F(x+t)-F(x)=h((x+t)^2)-h(x^2)=h(x^2)\big(h(2xt+t^2)-1\big)\). Since \(h(s)=1+sL(s)\) and \(L(s)\to1\), \(\dfrac{h(2xt+t^2)-1}{t}=(2x+t)L(2xt+t^2)\to2x\). Hence \(F'(x)=2x h(x^2)\).

4

Define \(g(x)=\ln(h(x))\). Prove that \(g\) is differentiable and find \(g'(x)\).

Since \(h(x)>0\), \(g\) is defined. Also \(g(x+t)-g(x)=\ln(h(x+t))-\ln(h(x))=\ln(h(t))\). Because \(h(t)=1+tL(t)\) and \(L(t)\to1\), \(\dfrac{\ln(h(t))}{t}\to1\). Thus \(g'(x)=1\).

Application

Piecewise rate: phone charge

Task

A phone company charges one dirham for the first 20 minutes of a call, then 10 fils per minute for the next 60 minutes, and 8 fils per minute for each additional minute. Let \(C(t)\) be the price in fils of a \(t\)-minute call, \(t>0\). Determine \(C'(t)\) as completely as possible.

Since \(1\) dirham \(=100\) fils,

\[C(t)=\begin{cases}100,&0<t\le20\\100+10(t-20),&20<t\le80\\700+8(t-80),&t>80\end{cases}\]

Thus \(C'(t)=0\) on \((0,20)\), \(C'(t)=10\) on \((20,80)\), and \(C'(t)=8\) on \((80,\infty)\). The derivative is not defined at \(t=20\) or \(t=80\).
