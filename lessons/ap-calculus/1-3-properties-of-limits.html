1.3 Properties of Limits

Use limit theorems to determine limits of sums, differences, products, quotients, powers, roots, and composite functions.

Teacher: Mohammad Abu Ghuwaleh
LimitsJustificationAlgebraic PropertiesAP Practice

Opening Question

If \(\lim_{x\to c} f(x)=3\) and \(\lim_{x\to c} g(x)=-2\), can we determine \(\lim_{x\to c}[f(x)+g(x)]\) without seeing the graphs?

Yes. Because both limits exist, the Sum Rule gives \(3+(-2)=1\).

Learning Outcomes

By the end, students can...

  • Use definitions, theorems, and properties to justify claims about limits.
  • Determine limits of functions using limit theorems.
  • Determine one-sided limits analytically or graphically.
  • Find limits of sums, differences, products, quotients, powers, roots, and composite functions.

Success Criteria

  • I write correct limit notation.
  • I name the property I am using.
  • I check that required limits exist before applying a property.
  • I identify traps such as a zero denominator or unequal one-sided limits.

Warm-Up: Which Claims Are Always True?

Discuss first. Then click to test.

Claim A

If \(\lim_{x\to c}f(x)=2\) and \(\lim_{x\to c}g(x)=5\), then \(\lim_{x\to c} f(x)g(x)=10\).

Always true. Product Rule.

Claim B

If \(\lim_{x\to c}f(x)=2\) and \(\lim_{x\to c}g(x)=0\), then \(\lim_{x\to c}\dfrac{f(x)}{g(x)}\) is \(0\).

False. The Quotient Rule requires the denominator limit to be nonzero.

Claim C

\(\lim_{x\to c}[f(x)+g(x)] = \lim_{x\to c}f(x)+\lim_{x\to c}g(x)\) whenever both individual limits exist.

Always true. Sum Rule.

Claim D

If \(\lim_{x\to c^-}f(x)=4\) and \(\lim_{x\to c^+}f(x)=4\), then \(\lim_{x\to c}f(x)=4\).

Always true. Limit Existence Theorem.

Why Properties Matter

Big Idea

Once we know limits of simple pieces, we can build limits of more complicated expressions.

\[\lim_{x\to c} f(x)=L, \quad \lim_{x\to c} g(x)=M\]

Then properties tell us what happens to combinations of \(f\) and \(g\).

Non-Negotiable AP Skill

Do not only compute. Justify.

A strong AP answer says both the value and the reason, for example: “Since \(\lim_{x\to c}f(x)\) and \(\lim_{x\to c}g(x)\) exist, the Product Rule applies.”

Limit Properties — The Theorem Bank

PropertyNotationStudent-Friendly Meaning
Constant\(\lim_{x\to c} b=b\)A constant stays the same.
Identity\(\lim_{x\to c} x=c\)As \(x\) approaches \(c\), \(x\) approaches \(c\).
Coefficient\(\lim_{x\to c}[b f(x)] = bL\)Constants can be pulled out of the limit.
Sum / Difference\(\lim_{x\to c}[f(x)\pm g(x)] = L\pm M\)The limit of a sum/difference is the sum/difference of the limits.
Product\(\lim_{x\to c}[f(x)g(x)] = LM\)Multiply the limits.
Quotient\(\lim_{x\to c}\dfrac{f(x)}{g(x)}=\dfrac{L}{M},\quad M\ne0\)Divide the limits only when the denominator limit is not zero.
Power\(\lim_{x\to c}[f(x)]^n=L^n\)Powers preserve limits when the inside limit exists.
Root\(\lim_{x\to c}\sqrt[n]{f(x)}=\sqrt[n]{L}\)The root must exist in the real numbers.
Composite\(\lim_{x\to c} f(g(x))=f\left(\lim_{x\to c}g(x)\right)\)Works when the outside function is continuous at the inside limit.

Activity 1: Say the Rule in English

Team Task

  1. Choose three properties from the theorem bank.
  2. Write each property in your own words.
  3. Create one example and one non-example for each.
  4. One student presents the rule. Another student gives the warning.

Example

Property: Quotient Rule

\[\lim_{x\to c}\frac{f(x)}{g(x)}=\frac{\lim f(x)}{\lim g(x)}\]

Warning: This is valid only if \(\lim_{x\to c}g(x)\ne 0\).

Guided Example: Build the Limit One Property at a Time

Suppose \(\lim_{x\to c}f(x)=3\), \(\lim_{x\to c}g(x)=-2\), and \(\lim_{x\to c}h(x)=4\).

\[\lim_{x\to c} \frac{3h(x)-2g(x)}{f(x)+5}\]
\[\frac{3\lim h(x)-2\lim g(x)}{\lim f(x)+5}=\frac{3(4)-2(-2)}{3+5}=\frac{16}{8}=2.\] Used: coefficient, difference, sum, and quotient rules. Denominator limit is \(8\ne0\), so quotient rule is valid.

Practice Activity 2: Theorem Relay

Given \(\lim_{x\to c}f(x)=3\), \(\lim_{x\to c}g(x)=-2\), and \(\lim_{x\to c}h(x)=4\). Work in pairs. One computes, one names the property.

A

\(\displaystyle \lim_{x\to c}\sqrt{3h(x)-2g(x)}\)

\(\sqrt{3(4)-2(-2)}=\sqrt{16}=4\).

B

\(\displaystyle \lim_{x\to c}[f(x)\cdot 5g(x)]\)

\(3\cdot 5(-2)=-30\).

C

\(\displaystyle \lim_{x\to c}[7-g(x)]^2\)

\([7-(-2)]^2=9^2=81\).

D

\(\displaystyle \lim_{x\to c}\frac{2f(x)+3h(x)}{h(x)-g(x)}\)

\(\frac{2(3)+3(4)}{4-(-2)}=\frac{18}{6}=3\).

Common Trap: The Quotient Rule Is Conditional

Incorrect Student Work

\[\lim_{x\to c}\frac{f(x)}{g(x)}=\frac{2}{0}=0\]

What is wrong?

Correct Reasoning

The quotient rule says

\[\lim_{x\to c}\frac{f(x)}{g(x)}=\frac{L}{M} \quad \text{only if } M\ne0.\]

If \(M=0\), the rule does not apply. The limit may be infinite, nonexistent, or require algebraic simplification.

One-Sided Limits and Properties

Properties also work for one-sided limits, but the side must stay consistent.

\[\lim_{x\to c^-}[f(x)+g(x)] = \lim_{x\to c^-}f(x)+\lim_{x\to c^-}g(x)\]
\[\lim_{x\to c^+}[f(x)g(x)] = \left(\lim_{x\to c^+}f(x)\right)\left(\lim_{x\to c^+}g(x)\right)\]

General limit exists only when left-hand and right-hand limits are equal.

Graph Activity: Read First, Then Combine

The graphs are redrawn clearly: first read the limit of each function at \(x=1\), then apply the property. Do not use the filled point unless the question asks for the function value.

Read First

  1. Find \(\displaystyle \lim_{x\to1^-} f(x)\) and \(\displaystyle \lim_{x\to1^+} f(x)\).
  2. Find \(\displaystyle \lim_{x\to1^-} g(x)\) and \(\displaystyle \lim_{x\to1^+} g(x)\).
  3. Compare the limits with \(f(1)\) and \(g(1)\).
\(\lim_{x\to1} f(x)=3\), while \(f(1)=-1\). Also, \(\lim_{x\to1} g(x)=1\), while \(g(1)=4\). Limits depend on nearby behavior, not the filled point.

Then Combine

  1. \(\displaystyle \lim_{x\to1}[f(x)+g(x)]\)
  2. \(\displaystyle \lim_{x\to1}[f(x)-2g(x)]\)
  3. \(\displaystyle \lim_{x\to1} f(x)g(x)\)
  4. \(\displaystyle \lim_{x\to1}\dfrac{f(x)}{g(x)}\)
  5. Tricky: Is \(\displaystyle \lim_{x\to1}[f(x)+g(x)] = f(1)+g(1)\)?
1) \(3+1=4\). 2) \(3-2(1)=1\). 3) \(3\cdot1=3\). 4) \(\dfrac31=3\). Tricky: No; \(f(1)+g(1)=-1+4=3\), but the limit of the sum is \(4\).

Graph Activity: Do the Properties Still Work?

This graph checks the hypotheses of the limit properties. If an individual limit does not exist, the property cannot be applied directly, even if a combined expression might still have a limit.

Check the Hypotheses

  1. Find \(\displaystyle \lim_{x\to2^-}f(x)\) and \(\displaystyle \lim_{x\to2^+}f(x)\).
  2. Does \(\displaystyle \lim_{x\to2}f(x)\) exist?
  3. Find \(\displaystyle \lim_{x\to2}g(x)\).
\(\lim_{x\to2^-}f(x)=4\) and \(\lim_{x\to2^+}f(x)=-1\), so \(\lim_{x\to2}f(x)\) does not exist. Also, \(\lim_{x\to2}g(x)=0\).

Combined Expressions

  1. \(\displaystyle \lim_{x\to2}[f(x)+g(x)]\)
  2. \(\displaystyle \lim_{x\to2}f(x)g(x)\)
  3. \(\displaystyle \lim_{x\to2}\dfrac{g(x)}{f(x)}\)
  4. Explain why answers 2 and 3 are not applications of the Product or Quotient Rule.
1) DNE because the one-sided sums approach \(4+0=4\) and \(-1+0=-1\). 2) \(0\), because both one-sided products approach \(0\). 3) \(0\), because \(\dfrac{g(x)}{f(x)}\to\dfrac04=0\) from the left and \(\dfrac0{-1}=0\) from the right. The formal product/quotient rules cannot be used directly because \(\lim f(x)\) does not exist.

Composite Function Rule

The Clean Case

If \(\lim_{x\to c}g(x)=M\) and \(f\) is continuous at \(M\), then

\[\lim_{x\to c} f(g(x))=f(M).\]

The Trap

You cannot always plug the inside limit into the outside function if the outside function has a break at that input.

\[\text{Need: } f \text{ continuous at } M.\]

Composition Practice from the Notes

A

Given \(f(x)=x^3-x^2+4x-1\) and \(g(x)=-2x\), find \(\displaystyle \lim_{x\to 2} f(g(x))\).

\(g(x)\to -4\). Since \(f\) is polynomial and continuous, \(f(-4)=(-4)^3-(-4)^2+4(-4)-1=-97\).

B

Given \(f(x)=x^3-x^2+4x-1\) and \(g(x)=-2x\), find \(\displaystyle \lim_{x\to 2} g(f(x))\).

\(f(2)=8-4+8-1=11\). Since \(g\) is continuous, \(g(11)=-22\).

Activity 3: Graphical Composition

For a composition, the output of the inside function becomes the input of the outside function.

Step-by-Step Composition

  1. Find \(\displaystyle \lim_{x\to2} f(x)\).
  2. Use that output as the new input and find \(\displaystyle \lim_{u\to1}g(u)\).
  3. Therefore find \(\displaystyle \lim_{x\to2}g(f(x))\).
The inside function approaches \(1\): \(\displaystyle \lim_{x\to2}f(x)=1\). The outside function approaches \(3\) as its input approaches \(1\): \(\displaystyle \lim_{u\to1}g(u)=3\). Therefore, \(\displaystyle \lim_{x\to2}g(f(x))=3\).

Tricky Questions

  1. Why does the filled point \(f(2)=4\) not control \(\displaystyle \lim_{x\to2}g(f(x))\)?
  2. Find \(g(f(2))\) from the graphs.
  3. Compare \(g(f(2))\) with \(\displaystyle \lim_{x\to2}g(f(x))\).
A limit uses nearby behavior, so \(f(x)\to1\), not \(f(2)=4\). Since \(f(2)=4\), \(g(f(2))=g(4)=\frac{3}{2}\) from the graph of \(g\). This is different from \(\displaystyle \lim_{x\to2}g(f(x))=3\).

Tricky Questions: Think Before Applying

Trick 1

If \(\lim_{x\to c}f(x)=0\) and \(\lim_{x\to c}g(x)\) DNE, must \(\lim_{x\to c} f(x)g(x)=0\)?

Not necessarily. The Product Rule cannot be used because \(\lim g(x)\) does not exist. It may be \(0\) in special cases, but it is not guaranteed.

Trick 2

Given \(\lim_{x\to c}f(x)=3\), determine \(\displaystyle \lim_{x\to c}\frac{[f(x)]^2-9}{f(x)-3}\).

The quotient rule gives \(0/0\), so it does not apply. If \(f(x)\ne3\) near \(c\), then \(\frac{f(x)^2-9}{f(x)-3}=f(x)+3\), so the limit is \(6\).

Trick 3

If \(\lim_{x\to c^-}f(x)=2\) and \(\lim_{x\to c^+}f(x)=-2\), determine \(\displaystyle \lim_{x\to c}[f(x)]^2\).

It exists and equals \(4\), because the squared one-sided limits are both \(4\).

Trick 4

If \(\lim_{x\to c}f(x)=4\), find \(\displaystyle \lim_{x\to c}\sqrt{f(x)-9}\).

No real limit can be concluded because the inside approaches \(-5\), and \(\sqrt{-5}\) is not real.

Challenge Set: Properties of Limits

Students solve in groups. Each answer must include a reason.

Challenge A

Suppose \(\lim_{x\to c}[f(x)+g(x)]=7\) and \(\lim_{x\to c}f(x)=2\). Find \(\lim_{x\to c}g(x)\).

\(5\), by the Difference Rule: \(g=(f+g)-f\).

Challenge B

If \(\lim_{x\to c}f(x)=L\), find \(\displaystyle \lim_{x\to c}\frac{[f(x)]^3-L^3}{f(x)-L}\), assuming \(f(x)\ne L\) near \(c\).

Factor: \(\frac{f^3-L^3}{f-L}=f^2+Lf+L^2\). Limit is \(L^2+L^2+L^2=3L^2\).

Challenge C

Can \(\lim_{x\to c} f(x)\) fail to exist while \(\lim_{x\to c}[f(x)]^2\) exists?

Yes. Example: left-hand limit \(2\), right-hand limit \(-2\). The original limit DNE, but the squared limit is \(4\).

Challenge D

Let \(\lim_{x\to c}g(x)=0\). What extra information would make \(\lim_{x\to c}\dfrac{f(x)}{g(x)}\) exist as a finite number?

You need more than limit properties. For example, algebraic cancellation or matching rates of approach could produce a finite limit.

AP Calculus Past-Paper Style Practice

AP-style MCQ

Let \(\lim_{x\to 2}f(x)=4\), \(\lim_{x\to 2}g(x)=-1\), and \(\lim_{x\to 2}h(x)=0\). Which expression has a limit that is guaranteed by limit properties?

A. \(\displaystyle \lim_{x\to2}\frac{f(x)}{h(x)}\)
B. \(\displaystyle \lim_{x\to2}\sqrt{f(x)-g(x)}\)
C. \(\displaystyle \lim_{x\to2}\frac{g(x)}{h(x)}\)
D. \(\displaystyle \lim_{x\to2}\frac{h(x)-1}{h(x)}\)
Correct answer: B. The inside approaches \(4-(-1)=5\), so the square root is real. The quotient options have denominator limit \(0\), so the quotient rule does not guarantee a finite limit.

This mirrors the reasoning style of official AP Calculus limit questions: apply the theorem only when the hypotheses are satisfied.

Summary

What We Learned

  • Limit properties let us build complex limits from simpler limits.
  • One-sided limits can use the same properties, but sides must match.
  • Quotient and root rules have conditions.
  • Composite limits require continuity of the outside function at the inside limit.

Final Sentence

Properties are powerful only when their hypotheses are true.

On AP Calculus, correct justification is part of the mathematics.
