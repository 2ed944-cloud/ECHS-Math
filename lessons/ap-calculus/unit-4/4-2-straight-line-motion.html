Connecting position, velocity, speed, acceleration, displacement, and distance

\[ v(t)=s'(t),\qquad a(t)=v'(t)=s''(t),\qquad \text{speed}=|v(t)| \]
The particle moves on a line or axis. It does not move along the drawn graph.

Learning targets and success criteria

Learning targets

  • Interpret derivatives in motion contexts.
  • Calculate rates of change from formulas, tables, and graphs.
  • Solve rectilinear-motion problems involving position, velocity, speed, and acceleration.
  • Analyze direction changes and speeding up or slowing down.

Success criteria

  • I distinguish signed quantities from nonnegative quantities.
  • I use signs of \(v\) and \(a\) together.
  • I separate displacement from total distance traveled.
  • I state units and justify every motion conclusion.
A correct derivative is only the beginning; the AP task is to explain what it says about the motion.

Opening diagnostic

A particle has position \(s(t)=t^3-6t^2+9t+2\) meters, where \(t\) is seconds.

Answers

A particle moves on the axis, not on the graph

What the graph means

  • The horizontal axis is time.
  • The vertical coordinate gives the particle's position on a separate motion line.
  • Positive graph slope means motion in the positive direction.
  • Negative graph slope means motion in the negative direction.
A rising position graph does not mean the path slopes upward in space. It means the coordinate is increasing with time.

Position depends on a reference point

Signed coordinate

\(s(t)=6\) means the particle is 6 units on the positive side of the origin. \(s(t)=-6\) means 6 units on the negative side.

Position is not distance traveled

A particle can return to its starting position after traveling a large distance. Then displacement is zero but distance traveled is positive.
A particle moves from position \(3\) to \(-5\), then to \(1\). Find displacement and distance traveled.
Answer
Displacement \(=1-3=-2\). Distance traveled \(=|-5-3|+|1-(-5)|=8+6=14\).

Displacement versus distance traveled

Displacement

\[ \Delta s=s(b)-s(a) \]

Signed net change; it may be positive, negative, or zero.

Total distance

Split at every direction change and add absolute position changes:

\[ |s(t_1)-s(a)|+\cdots+|s(b)-s(t_k)|. \]
Never use \(|s(b)-s(a)|\) as total distance unless the particle never reverses direction.

Average velocity

\[ v_{\mathrm{avg}}=\frac{s(b)-s(a)}{b-a} =\frac{\text{displacement}}{\text{elapsed time}}. \]
A falling book has height \(s(t)=100-16t^2\) feet. Find its average velocity on \([1,2]\).
Interpret the sign and distinguish the answer from average speed.
Answer
\(\dfrac{s(2)-s(1)}{1}=\dfrac{36-84}{1}=-48\) ft/s. Over this interval its height decreases on average by 48 ft each second. Because it moves only downward on the interval, average speed is \(48\) ft/s.

Instantaneous velocity

Derivative definition

\[ v(t)=s'(t)=\lim_{h\to0}\frac{s(t+h)-s(t)}h. \]

Interpretation

Velocity is the tangent slope of the position graph. Its sign indicates direction, and its magnitude is speed.
For \(s(t)=64-16t^2\), find and interpret \(v(2)\).
Answer
\(v(t)=-32t\), so \(v(2)=-64\) ft/s. At 2 seconds, the object's height is decreasing at 64 ft/s; it moves downward with speed 64 ft/s.

Speed is magnitude, not direction

\[ \boxed{\text{speed}=|v(t)|} \]

\(v=7\)

Moves right/up at speed 7.

\(v=-7\)

Moves left/down at speed 7.

\(v=0\)

Instantaneously at rest.
Speed cannot be negative. A negative velocity means direction, not “negative fastness.”

Acceleration

\[ a(t)=v'(t)=s''(t)=\frac{d^2s}{dt^2}. \]
Acceleration tells how velocity changes—not automatically how speed changes. Positive acceleration means velocity is increasing; negative acceleration means velocity is decreasing.
If \(v(4)=-12\) m/s and \(a(4)=3\) m/s\(^2\), interpret both values.
Answer
At 4 seconds the particle moves in the negative direction at speed 12 m/s. Its velocity is increasing by 3 m/s each second; because velocity is negative while acceleration is positive, the speed is decreasing.

Direction comes from velocity

\(v(t)>0\)

Moving right or upward—the chosen positive direction.

\(v(t)<0\)

Moving left or downward—the chosen negative direction.

\(v(t)=0\)

At rest at that instant; check signs around the time before claiming reversal.
Position sign tells where the particle is. Velocity sign tells which way it is moving. These are different questions.

Speeding up or slowing down

\(v>0,\ a>0\)speeding up
\(v>0,\ a<0\)slowing down
\(v<0,\ a<0\)speeding up
\(v<0,\ a>0\)slowing down

Fast sign test

\[ v(t)a(t)>0\Rightarrow\text{speeding up},\qquad v(t)a(t)<0\Rightarrow\text{slowing down}. \]
Why
Speed increases when velocity and its rate of change point in the same signed direction. It decreases when acceleration pushes velocity toward zero.

At rest does not always mean direction change

Stops and reverses

\[ v(t)=(t-2) \]

Velocity changes from negative to positive at \(t=2\).

Stops but continues same way

\[ v(t)=(t-2)^2 \]

Velocity is nonnegative on both sides of \(t=2\).

Solve \(v(t)=0\), then make a sign chart. A zero alone does not prove a reversal.

A direction change can occur without velocity being defined

A particle changes direction whenever the sign of velocity changes—from positive to negative or negative to positive.
\[ s(t)=|t-2| \]

At \(t=2\), velocity is not defined, yet the motion changes from left to right.

AP reasoning

Do not require \(v(c)=0\) if \(v(c)\) does not exist. Check the sign of velocity on the intervals around \(c\).

Acceleration zero: what it does and does not mean

\(a(c)=0\)

Velocity has a horizontal tangent at \(t=c\). It may have a local maximum, local minimum, or neither.

It does not mean

  • the particle is at rest;
  • speed is zero;
  • position is greatest;
  • velocity is constant for an interval.
When a question says “maximum velocity,” solve \(a(t)=0\), then verify the change in \(a\) or behavior of \(v\).

Workflow from a position formula

Always restrict answers to the physical domain, usually \(t\ge0\), and reject negative or nonreal times.

Displacement and distance from a formula

A particle has \(s(t)=t^3-8t^2+20t-16\) meters for \(0\le t\le3\).
  1. Find \(v(t)\) and \(a(t)\).
  2. Find all rest times in \([0,3]\).
  1. Find displacement on \([0,3]\).
  2. Find total distance on \([0,3]\).
Solutions
\(v=3t^2-16t+20=(3t-10)(t-2)\), \(a=6t-16\). In \([0,3]\), rest occurs at \(t=2\) only; \(10/3\) is outside. Displacement \(s(3)-s(0)=(-1)-(-16)=15\) m. Since \(v>0\) on \((0,2)\) and \(v<0\) on \((2,3)\), distance \(=|s(2)-s(0)|+|s(3)-s(2)|=|0-(-16)|+|-1-0|=17\) m.

Average acceleration

\[ a_{\mathrm{avg}}=\frac{v(b)-v(a)}{b-a}. \]
For the main velocity graph, find average acceleration on \([0,4]\).
Find average acceleration on \([4,10]\) and explain why this does not mean acceleration is constant.
Answers
On \([0,4]\): \((0-(-10))/4=2.5\) units/s\(^2\). On \([4,10]\): \((0-0)/6=0\). The latter is zero because initial and final velocities match; the graph clearly shows positive, zero, and negative acceleration inside the interval.

Rolle's Theorem applied to velocity

Because velocity has equal endpoint values, there exists \(c\in(7,12)\) such that \[ v'(c)=a(c)=0. \]
A table alone does not locate \(c\). It guarantees existence only when continuity/differentiability conditions are given.

Geometric displacement from a velocity graph

When regions are simple shapes

Signed area below the \(t\)-axis counts negatively for displacement. For total distance, use absolute areas.

\[ \Delta s=\text{signed geometric area},\qquad \text{distance}=\text{sum of absolute areas}. \]
Main-graph values

Free-fall model

Ignoring air resistance; upward is positive

\[ s(t)=s_0+v_0t-\frac12gt^2,\qquad v(t)=v_0-gt,\qquad a(t)=-g. \]

Earth

\(g\approx9.8\text{ m/s}^2\) or \(32\text{ ft/s}^2\).

Moon example

The source models lunar acceleration as \(1.6\text{ m/s}^2\), giving \(s=s_0+v_0t-0.8t^2\).
A different sign convention is valid, but position, velocity, and acceleration must all use it consistently.

Vertical-motion interpretation

Rising

\(v>0\), while gravity gives \(a<0\): the object slows down.

Highest point

\(v=0\), but \(a=-g\ne0\). It is instantaneously at rest.

Falling

\(v<0\) and \(a<0\): the object speeds up downward.
The highest point is found from \(v(t)=0\), not \(s(t)=0\). Ground impact is found by setting height equal to ground level.

Common motion errors

1
“\(v<0\), so speed is negative.”
2
“\(a>0\), so the particle speeds up.”
3
“\(v=0\), so direction changes.”
4
“\(a=0\), so the particle is at rest.”
5
“Distance equals \(s(b)-s(a)\).”
6
“At the highest point, acceleration is zero.”
Corrections
Speed is \(|v|\); acceleration sign must be compared with velocity; direction changes require a sign change in \(v\); rest means \(v=0\); \(s(b)-s(a)\) is displacement; and gravity remains nonzero at the highest point.

Tricky reasoning set

A
Can a particle move left while its velocity is increasing?
B
Can speed be decreasing while acceleration is positive?
C
Can displacement be zero while distance is positive?
D
Can direction change where velocity is undefined?
Answers
Yes to all. A and B occur when \(v<0<a\). C occurs after a round trip. D occurs at a corner such as \(s(t)=|t-c|\), where one-sided velocity signs differ.

Parameter challenge

A particle has velocity \(v(t)=t^2-kt+6\), \(t\ge0\).
  1. Find \(k\) so the particle is at rest exactly once.
  2. Does it change direction then?
  1. Find the rest time.
  2. Find acceleration there.
Solutions
Exactly one zero requires discriminant \(k^2-24=0\). For a nonnegative rest time use \(k=2\sqrt6\), giving \(v=(t-\sqrt6)^2\). Rest occurs at \(t=\sqrt6\), but velocity stays nonnegative, so no direction change. \(a=2t-k\), so \(a(\sqrt6)=0\).

Translated Arabic resource: physical foundations

Derivative meanings

  • Instantaneous velocity is the first derivative of position.
  • Acceleration is the first derivative of velocity and second derivative of position.
  • Terms such as “maximum height,” “minimum position,” “momentarily stops,” and “turns around” signal that velocity may be zero.
  • “Greatest or least velocity” signals that acceleration may be zero.

Physical restrictions

  • Time is nonnegative and must be real.
  • Velocity sign determines direction.
  • Acceleration sign alone does not determine speeding up.
  • Distance traveled is nonnegative; displacement may have any sign.
  • At the launch instant, use \(t=0\) and the initial position and velocity.
All ideas on the Arabic summary page have been translated and reorganized here using standard AP Calculus notation.

Translated Arabic resource: vertical sign convention

Upward positive

\[ s=s_0+v_0t-\frac12gt^2,\quad a=-g. \]

Rising velocity is positive; falling velocity is negative.

Downward positive

\[ d=d_0+u_0t+\frac12gt^2,\quad a=+g. \]

Downward distance and velocity are positive.
