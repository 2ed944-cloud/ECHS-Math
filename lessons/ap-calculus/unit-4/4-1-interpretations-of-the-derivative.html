Learning targets and success criteria

Learning targets

  • Interpret a derivative in a real-world context.
  • Connect tangent slope and instantaneous rate of change.
  • Use derivative notation with meaningful variables.
  • Determine derivative units from the dependent and independent variables.

I can show mastery when...

  • My sentence names the quantity, input value, direction, rate, and units.
  • I distinguish \(f(a)\), \(f'(a)\), and an average rate.
  • I recognize when the output is already a rate and the derivative is a rate of a rate.
  • I avoid conclusions not justified by one derivative value.
Precision matters: a correct number with an incorrect meaning or incorrect units is not a complete interpretation.

Opening diagnostic: what does each quantity tell us?

The temperature \(T\), in degrees Celsius, of soup is a differentiable function of time \(t\), in minutes.
A
\(T(6)=71\)
B
\(T'(6)=-2.4\)
C
\(\dfrac{T(8)-T(4)}{8-4}=-2.1\)
D
\(T'(6)=0\)
For each: Is it a value, an instantaneous rate, or an average rate? Give its units and one valid conclusion.
Answers
  1. A is a value: 6 minutes after observation begins, the soup is \(71^\circ\text{C}\).
  2. B is instantaneous: at \(t=6\), the temperature is decreasing at \(2.4^\circ\text{C}/\text{min}\).
  3. C is average: from 4 to 8 minutes, the temperature decreases on average by \(2.1^\circ\text{C}/\text{min}\).
  4. D says the instantaneous temperature change is zero at 6 minutes; it does not by itself prove a maximum, minimum, or constant temperature.

Function value versus derivative value

QuantityWhat it representsIf \(f\) is gallons and \(x\) is minutes

Do not blur the levels. \(f'(a)\) describes how \(f\) changes; \(f''(a)\) describes how the rate \(f'\) changes.
Quick check
If \(V'(4)=-3\), the volume is decreasing at 3 gallons per minute at \(t=4\). If \(V''(4)=-3\), the rate \(V'\) is decreasing by 3 gallons per minute each minute at \(t=4\).

Derivative notation: one idea in several forms

Equivalent notations

\[ f'(a)=\left.\frac{df}{dx}\right|_{x=a} =\left.\frac{dy}{dx}\right|_{x=a} =\left.\frac{d}{dx}[f(x)]\right|_{x=a}. \]

Read it aloud

“The derivative of \(f\) with respect to \(x\), evaluated at \(x=a\).”

The vertical bar means evaluate the derivative at this input.
If \(V\) is tank volume in gallons and \(t\) is time in minutes, write “the instantaneous rate of change of volume with respect to time at \(t=3\)” in two forms.
Answers
\(V'(3)\) and \(\left.\dfrac{dV}{dt}\right|_{t=3}\).

Use meaningful variables

AP free-response work should preserve the variables from the context. Writing \(\frac{dy}{dx}\) when the problem defines \(C\) as a function of \(n\) hides meaning.

Clear

If \(C\) is cost and \(n\) is number of passengers, \[ \frac{dC}{dn}=C'(n). \] Its units are dollars per passenger.

Too generic

\[ \frac{dy}{dx}=58 \] does not identify what changes, with respect to what, or the correct contextual units.
Write the most meaningful derivative notation when population \(P\) depends on years \(t\), and force \(F\) depends on height \(h\).
Answers
\(\frac{dP}{dt}=P'(t)\) and \(\frac{dF}{dh}=F'(h)\).

From average change to instantaneous change

\(s(t)=(t+1)^2\)secantstangent

Average rate

\[ \frac{s(2)-s(2-h)}{h} \] is the slope of a secant line over a nonzero interval.

Instantaneous rate

\[ s'(2)=\lim_{h\to0}\frac{s(2)-s(2-h)}{h}=6. \] It is the slope of the tangent line at \(t=2\).
Graph check
For \(s(t)=(t+1)^2\), \(s'(t)=2(t+1)\), so \(s'(2)=6\). The tangent drawn is \(y=6(t-2)+9=6t-3\). Secant slopes from \(t=0.5,1,1.5\) to \(t=2\) are \(4.5,5,\) and \(5.5\), approaching \(6\).

If \(F(h)\) is measured in newtons and \(h\) in meters, what are the units of \(F'(500)\)?
Answer
Newtons per meter, \(\text{N}/\text{m}\). The derivative measures how force changes with height, not force itself.

Units as a dimensional detector

The depth \(d(t)\) of water is measured in feet, with \(t\) in hours. Decide whether each sentence could describe \(d'(4)=-0.7\).
A. At 4 hours, the depth is decreasing at \(0.7\) foot per hour.
B. At 4 hours, the depth is \(-0.7\) foot.
C. During the first 4 hours, the depth decreased by \(0.7\) foot.
D. At 4 hours, the water has a velocity of \(-0.7\) foot.
Answers
Only A. B uses output units instead of derivative units. C is a total change over an interval, not an instantaneous rate. D omits “per hour” and introduces velocity without justification.

Sign gives direction

\(f'(a)>0\)

At \(x=a\), \(f\) is increasing locally.

\(f'(a)<0\)

At \(x=a\), \(f\) is decreasing locally.

\(f'(a)=0\)

The tangent is horizontal; more information is needed.
Use “increasing at a rate of \(L\)” when \(L>0\), and “decreasing at a rate of \(|L|\)” when \(L<0\). Do not say “decreasing at a rate of \(-3\).”
If \(P'(7)=-420\), write a precise direction-and-rate phrase.
Answer
At input 7, \(P\) is decreasing at a rate of 420 output-units per input-unit. The contextual nouns and units must be added from the problem.

Reading sign from a graph

The water depth is

\[ d(t)=6+0.8t-0.12t^2, \] where \(d\) is in feet and \(t\) is in hours.
  1. Interpret \(d'(2)\).
  2. Interpret \(d'(6)\).
  3. At what time is the depth instantaneously not changing?
Answers
\(d'(t)=0.8-0.24t\). Thus \(d'(2)=0.32\) ft/hr: at 2 hours, depth is increasing at 0.32 ft/hr. \(d'(6)=-0.64\) ft/hr: at 6 hours, depth is decreasing at 0.64 ft/hr. \(d'(t)=0\) at \(t=10/3\) hours.

The complete interpretation sentence

Reliable AP sentence frame

At [input value + input unit], the [dependent quantity] is [increasing/decreasing] at an instantaneous rate of [magnitude + output unit per input unit].
Let \(A(t)\) be the amount of medication, in milligrams, in a patient's bloodstream \(t\) hours after a dose. Interpret \(A'(3)=-4.6\).
Model answer
Three hours after the dose, the amount of medication in the patient's bloodstream is decreasing at an instantaneous rate of \(4.6\) milligrams per hour.
Every word earns its place: time, quantity, direction, magnitude, and units.

Five common interpretation errors

  1. Missing the input: saying only “the population grows.”
  2. Missing “per” units: writing people instead of people/year.
  3. Confusing value and rate: treating \(f'(a)\) as \(f(a)\).
  4. Using an interval: saying “from 0 to \(a\)” for an instantaneous statement.
  5. Overclaiming: concluding global behavior from one derivative value.
Correct this response:

“\(T'(5)=-3\) means that during the first five minutes the pizza lost \(3^\circ\text{F}\).”
Correction
Five minutes after the pizza was removed from the oven, its temperature is decreasing at an instantaneous rate of \(3^\circ\text{F}\) per minute. The derivative does not state the total change over the first five minutes.

Worked example: draining a fish tank

The volume \(V\), in gallons, of water in a fish tank is a differentiable function of time \(t\), in minutes. Interpret \[ \left.\frac{dV}{dt}\right|_{t=3}=-2. \]

Decode

  • Input: time \({}=3\) minutes
  • Output: volume of water
  • Sign: negative, so decreasing
  • Magnitude: \(2\)
  • Units: gallons/minute
Model answer
Three minutes after draining begins, the volume of water in the tank is decreasing at an instantaneous rate of 2 gallons per minute.

Worked example: water balloon motion

Let \(s(t)\) be the distance, in feet, traveled by a water balloon \(t\) seconds after launch. Suppose \[ s'(2)=38\ \frac{\text{ft}}{\text{s}}. \]

Meaning

At exactly 2 seconds, the balloon's distance from its starting point is increasing at 38 feet per second.

Not implied

  • The balloon traveled 38 feet total.
  • Its average speed from 0 to 2 seconds was 38 ft/s.
  • Its acceleration was 38 ft/s2.

Write the derivative from words

1
The total extraction cost \(C(b)\), in dollars, depends on the number \(b\) of barrels. At 600 barrels, total cost is decreasing by 3 dollars per additional barrel.
2
The leaked volume \(V(t)\), in cubic meters, is increasing at 790 cubic meters per day at the end of day 7.
3
The gorilla population \(P(t)\) is increasing at 114 gorillas per year at \(t=11\), where \(t\) is years since 1992.
4
At a height of 500 meters, force \(F(h)\) is increasing by 0.2 newton per meter.
Answers
1. \(C'(600)=-3\). 2. \(V'(7)=790\). 3. \(P'(11)=114\). 4. \(F'(500)=0.2\). Each derivative uses output units per input unit.

A subtle language trap: total change is not a derivative

“The population increased by 114 during 2003” gives a change over a year. It does not by itself give the instantaneous derivative at one moment.

What is justified

If \(t\) is years since 1992, \[ P(12)-P(11)=114 \] and the average rate over that year is \(114\) gorillas/year.

What needs extra information

\[ P'(11)=114 \] would require a statement about the instantaneous rate at \(t=11\), not merely the year's total increase.
Teacher note
This distinction is a common AP trap: finite changes belong to difference quotients; derivatives describe limiting instantaneous change.

Position, velocity, and direction

When position is the output

If \(s(t)\) is position, then \(s'(t)\) is velocity. The sign of velocity gives direction relative to the chosen positive direction.
A car's position \(s(t)\), in miles east of a checkpoint, satisfies \(s'(2)=-54\). Interpret this statement.
A student's distance \(D(t)\), in miles traveled along a route, satisfies \(D'(2)=54\). Interpret this statement. Why is the wording different?
Answers
For position, at 2 hours the car is moving west at 54 mph because east was defined positive. For total route distance, at 2 hours the distance traveled is increasing at 54 mph; distance does not encode west/east direction.

Velocity, acceleration, and a rate of a rate

A particle's velocity is \[ v(t)=12+4t-t^2 \] in meters per second, with \(t\) in seconds.
  1. Interpret \(v(3)\).
  2. Interpret \(v'(3)\).
  3. State the units of each.
Answers
\(v(3)=15\) m/s: at 3 seconds, the particle moves in the positive direction at 15 m/s. \(v'(t)=4-2t\), so \(v'(3)=-2\) m/s2: at 3 seconds, velocity is decreasing by 2 m/s each second. Since \(v(3)>0\) and \(v'(3)<0\), the particle is slowing down at that instant.

When the function is already a rate

The water-flow rate \(q(t)\) is measured in gallons per second, where \(t\) is measured in seconds. Interpret each quantity.

\(q(5)=200\)flow rate at 5 secondsgal/s

If the dependent variable's units already contain “per,” differentiating adds another division by the input unit.
Model interpretation
\(q'(5)=200\) means that at 5 seconds, the water-flow rate is increasing by 200 gallons per second each second.

Marginal cost is a derivative

The total cost \(C(n)\), in dollars, of an airline flight is modeled as a differentiable function of the number \(n\) of passengers. Interpret \[ C'(182)=58. \]

Continuous model, discrete input

Although passenger count is discrete, the model's derivative gives a local approximation: \[ C(183)-C(182)\approx C'(182)(1)=58. \]
Model answer
When there are 182 passengers, the total flight cost is increasing at approximately 58 dollars per additional passenger. The model predicts that adding the 183rd passenger increases total cost by about 58 dollars.

Marginal cost wording trap

The total production cost is \(C(s)\), where \(s\) is the number of scooters. Compare the statements.

\(C'(400)=27.50\)

Near 400 scooters, total cost increases by about \(\$27.50\) per additional scooter.

\(C''(400)=27.50\)

Near 400 scooters, the marginal cost itself increases by about \(\$27.50\) per scooter per additional scooter.
Which derivative matches: “At 400 scooters, the cost of producing one more scooter is increasing as production rises”?
Answer
The phrase describes a change in marginal cost, so it points to \(C''(400)>0\). If it states that the marginal cost is exactly \(\$27.50\), then \(C'(400)=27.50\).

Medication dosage and local prediction

The recommended antibiotic dose \(D(w)\), in milligrams, is a differentiable function of patient weight \(w\), in pounds. Suppose \[ D(135)=120,\qquad D'(135)=2.5. \]

Interpretation

At 135 lb, recommended dose is increasing at 2.5 mg per additional pound.

Estimate \(D(140)\)

\[ D(140)\approx D(135)+D'(135)(5)=132.5\text{ mg}. \]
Why this works
The tangent line gives a local linear approximation. The derivative is not itself the dose; it converts a small weight change into an estimated dose change.

Cooling pizza: sign, units, and scope

The pizza temperature \(T(t)\), in degrees Fahrenheit, is a differentiable function of minutes \(t\) after removal from an oven. Suppose \(T'(5)=-3\).

What follows

  • At 5 minutes, temperature is decreasing.
  • The instantaneous rate is \(3^\circ\text{F}/\text{min}\).
  • For a very small \(\Delta t\), \(\Delta T\approx-3\Delta t\).

What does not follow

  • \(T(5)=-3\)
  • Total decrease during 5 minutes is \(3^\circ\text{F}\)
  • Cooling rate is \(-3\) for all time
  • The pizza reaches a minimum at \(t=5\)

Population units may hide a scale factor

The population \(P(t)\) of a city is measured in thousands of people, with \(t\) in years since 2010. Suppose \(P'(4)=3\).

Model units

\[ 3\ \frac{\text{thousand people}}{\text{year}} \]

Ordinary units

\[ 3000\ \frac{\text{people}}{\text{year}} \]
Model interpretation
In 2014, the city's population is increasing at an instantaneous rate of 3 thousand people per year, or 3,000 people per year.

Local linearity is built into the interpretation

For small changes near \(x=a\)

\[ \Delta f\approx f'(a)\Delta x, \qquad f(a+\Delta x)\approx f(a)+f'(a)\Delta x. \]
If \(R'(6)=0.5\) cm/hr for cumulative rainfall and \(R(6)=4.8\) cm, estimate \(R(6.2)\).
If \(F'(500)=0.2\) N/m and \(F(500)=3.2\) N, estimate \(F(503)\).
Answers
\(R(6.2)\approx4.8+0.5(0.2)=4.9\) cm. \(F(503)\approx3.2+0.2(3)=3.8\) N. These are local estimates, not exact values unless the functions are linear.

Table choice changes the quality of an estimate

To estimate \(f'(3)\), which quotient is usually most appropriate when all values are available?
\(x\)2.62.93.03.13.8
\(f(x)\)7.128.418.889.3913.92
A. \(\dfrac{f(3)-f(2.9)}{0.1}\)
B. \(\dfrac{f(3.1)-f(2.9)}{0.2}\)
C. \(\dfrac{f(3.8)-f(2.6)}{1.2}\)
Answer
B is generally best because it uses nearby points on both sides of 3. It gives \((9.39-8.41)/0.2=4.9\). A is one-sided; C averages over a much wider interval.

Interpret a table-based derivative estimate

The mass \(m(t)\), in grams, of a dissolving tablet is recorded \(t\) minutes after it enters water.
\(t\)4.55.05.5
\(m(t)\)18.717.115.8
  1. Estimate \(m'(5)\).
  2. Interpret the sign and units.
  3. Use the estimate to predict \(m(5.1)\).
Answers
\(m'(5)\approx(15.8-18.7)/(5.5-4.5)=-2.9\) g/min. At 5 minutes, mass is decreasing at approximately 2.9 g/min. \(m(5.1)\approx17.1-2.9(0.1)=16.81\) g.

Interpret a derivative limit in context

The amount \(A(t)\), in liters, of fuel in a tank \(t\) hours after noon is differentiable. Interpret \[ \lim_{h\to0}\frac{A(4+h)-A(4)}{h}=-7.2. \]

Recognition

The limit is \(A'(4)\).

Units

\[ \frac{\text{liters}}{\text{hour}}. \]
Model answer
At 4:00 p.m., the amount of fuel in the tank is decreasing at an instantaneous rate of 7.2 liters per hour.

Difference quotient: interpret the entire expression

The population \(P(t)\), in thousands, is modeled \(t\) years after 2020. Explain the meaning and units of \[ \frac{P(8)-P(3)}{8-3}=1.7. \]

Correct

From 2023 to 2028, population increased on average by 1.7 thousand people per year.

Incorrect

At \(t=8\), population is increasing instantaneously by 1.7 thousand people per year.
Why
The quotient uses two distinct times, so it is an average rate over an interval. Only a limit of such quotients at one input becomes the derivative.

Amount versus rate function

Amount function

\(W(t)\) is gallons of water in a tank.
  • \(W'(t)\): gal/min
  • \(W''(t)\): gal/min2

Rate function

\(r(t)\) is the inflow rate in gal/min.
  • \(r'(t)\): gal/min2
  • The amount added from \(a\) to \(b\) is not \(r'(b)\).
If \(W'(4)=-8\) and \(r'(4)=-8\), write one correct sentence for each.
Answers
\(W'(4)=-8\): at 4 minutes the water amount is decreasing at 8 gal/min. \(r'(4)=-8\): at 4 minutes the inflow rate is decreasing by 8 gal/min each minute.

Interpreting an inverse derivative in context

Rainfall \(R(t)\), in centimeters, is a one-to-one differentiable function of hours after midnight. Suppose \[ R^{-1}(5)=14,\qquad (R^{-1})'(5)=1.5. \]

Inverse value

Five centimeters of rain has accumulated by 14 hours after midnight.

Inverse derivative

Near a rainfall total of 5 cm, the time required changes at 1.5 hours per additional centimeter.
Connection
Since \(R(14)=5\), \((R^{-1})'(5)=1/R'(14)=1.5\), so \(R'(14)=2/3\) cm/hr.

Graph-based interpretation: position

A particle's position is \[ s(t)=t^3-6t^2+9t+2 \] meters at time \(t\) seconds.
  1. Interpret \(s'(2)\).
  2. At what times is the particle instantaneously at rest?
  3. Is the particle moving left or right at \(t=2\)?
Answers
\(s'(t)=3t^2-12t+9=3(t-1)(t-3)\). Thus \(s'(2)=-3\) m/s: at 2 seconds, position is decreasing at 3 m/s, so the particle moves in the negative direction. It is at rest at \(t=1\) and \(t=3\).

Graph-based interpretation: tangent data

A population \(P(t)\), in thousands, is modeled \(t\) years after 2020 by \[ P(t)=40+5t-0.4t^2. \]
  1. Find and interpret \(P'(4)\).
  2. Use the tangent line to estimate \(P(4.25)\).
  3. Explain why the estimate need not be exact.
Answers
\(P'(t)=5-0.8t\), so \(P'(4)=1.8\) thousand people/year. In 2024, population is increasing at 1,800 people/year. \(P(4)=53.6\), so \(P(4.25)\approx53.6+1.8(0.25)=54.05\) thousand. The graph is curved, while the tangent model is linear.

AP-style multiple choice: limit and units

The mass \(M(t)\), in kilograms, of recycled material collected \(t\) days after a campaign begins satisfies \[ \lim_{h\to0}\frac{M(12+h)-M(12)}h=4.7. \] Which statement is best?
A. During the first 12 days, 4.7 kg was collected.
B. On day 12, total mass is 4.7 kg.
C. At day 12, collected mass is increasing at 4.7 kg/day.
D. From day 12 onward, exactly 4.7 kg/day will be collected.
Answer
C. The limit is \(M'(12)\), an instantaneous rate. It does not describe the entire first interval or guarantee a constant future rate.

The zero-derivative trap

All three functions satisfy \(f'(0)=0\), but their behavior at 0 is different.

\(f(x)=x^2\)

Local minimum at 0.

\(g(x)=-x^2\)

Local maximum at 0.

\(h(x)=x^3\)

Neither maximum nor minimum at 0.
\[ f'(a)=0\quad\Longrightarrow\quad\text{horizontal tangent only.} \]

Ultra challenge: identify every impossible claim

Let \(A(t)\), in liters, be differentiable with \(t\) in hours, and suppose \(A'(3)=-5\). Which claims are guaranteed, possible but not guaranteed, or impossible?
1. \(A(3)=-5\) liters.
2. At 3 hours, amount decreases at 5 L/hr.
3. \(A\) has decreased by 15 liters from 0 to 3.
4. \(A\) has a local minimum at 3.
5. For sufficiently small positive \(h\), \(A(3+h)<A(3)\).
6. \(A(3.01)\approx A(3)-0.05\).
Answers
Guaranteed: 2, and 5 follows from the negative derivative. The local approximation in 6 is justified, though not exact. Claim 3 is possible but not guaranteed.
