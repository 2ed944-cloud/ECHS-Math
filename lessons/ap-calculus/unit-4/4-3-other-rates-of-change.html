Learning targets and success criteria

Learning targets

  • Interpret rates of change in applied contexts.
  • Calculate instantaneous rates from formulas, tables, and graphs.
  • Distinguish an amount from a rate and a rate from a rate of a rate.
  • Use derivatives in economics, biology, chemistry, and physics.

Success criteria

  • I name what is changing and with respect to what variable.
  • I attach correct compound units.
  • I use the sign of a derivative to justify increasing or decreasing behavior.
  • I recognize when a model gives an impossible or questionable contextual prediction.
Every strong AP response contains a number, units, a contextual noun, and a time or input value.

Opening diagnostic

The amount of medicine in a patient is modeled by \(M(t)=40e^{-0.18t}\) milligrams, where \(t\) is measured in hours.
A
What does \(M(3)\) represent?
B
What does \(M'(3)\) represent?
C
What are the units of \(M''(3)\)?
D
Is the amount increasing or decreasing at \(t=3\)?
Answers
\(M(3)\) is the amount present after 3 hours. \(M'(3)\) is the instantaneous change in that amount, in mg/hour. \(M''(3)\) has units mg/hour\(^2\). Since \(M'(3)=-7.2e^{-0.54}<0\), the amount is decreasing.

Interpretation template

At input \(x=a\), the [output quantity] is increasing/decreasing at approximately \(f'(a)\) [output units per input unit].
Do not write “the function is changing by...” unless you identify the actual contextual quantity.

The unit rule

Derivative units

If \(y=f(x)\), then the units of \(f'(x)\) are \[ \frac{\text{units of }y}{\text{units of }x}. \] Each additional derivative divides by the input unit one more time.
FunctionUnitsDerivativeDerivative units
\(T(t)\)°F\(T'(t)\)°F/day
\(R(t)\)gallons/min\(R'(t)\)gallons/min\(^2\)
\(C(x)\)dollars\(C'(x)\)dollars/item

Units as a dimensional detector

Pond evaporation

If \(t=f(P)\), where \(t\) is days and \(P\) is liters, then \[ f'(P):\ \frac{\text{days}}{\text{liter}},\qquad f''(P):\ \frac{\text{days}}{\text{liter}^2}. \]

Production rate

If \(R(t)\) is items/hour, then \[ R'(t):\ \frac{\text{items/hour}}{\text{hour}}=\frac{\text{items}}{\text{hour}^2}. \]
Writing “items per hour” for \(R'(t)\) loses one time unit and changes the meaning.

Sign gives local behavior

\(f'(a)>0\)output is increasing
\(f'(a)<0\)output is decreasing
\(f'(a)=0\)instantaneously unchanged
\(f''(a)<0\)the rate \(f'\) is decreasing
“Increasing” and “positive” are different. A quantity may be negative and increasing, or positive and decreasing.

Build a complete interpretation sentence

Suppose \(W'(10)=0.4\), where \(W(t)\) is lake temperature in °F and \(t\) is days.
Incomplete:
“It is increasing at 0.4.”
Complete:
“On day 10, the lake temperature is increasing at approximately \(0.4\) °F per day.”
Include the time, the contextual quantity, the direction, the numerical value, and the compound units.

Is the function already a rate?

Population amount

If \(P(t)\) is the number of rabbits, then \(P'(t)\) is the rabbit population growth rate.

Population growth rate

If \(G(t)\) is already rabbits/year, then \(G'(t)\) tells how the growth rate changes, in rabbits/year\(^2\).
Before differentiating, read the units printed beside the original function.

Interpreting the derivative of a rate

A factory produces items at rate \(R(t)\) items/hour. Suppose \(R'(4)=-18\).

Correct interpretation

Four hours after production begins, the production rate is decreasing at \(18\) items per hour per hour.
Not: the factory is losing 18 items/hour.
Not: total production is decreasing by 18 items.

Derivative on a calculator

Numerical derivative

For \(f(x)=5^{\sin x}\), evaluate \[ f'(3)=\left.\frac{d}{dx}5^{\sin x}\right|_{x=3}. \] A calculator gives approximately \(-1.983\).

Analytic check

\[ f'(x)=5^{\sin x}\ln(5)\cos x, \] so \(f'(3)=5^{\sin3}\ln(5)\cos3\approx-2.000\).
A calculator value still needs context and units when the problem is applied.

Museum entering and exiting

People enter a museum at \(f(t)\) people/hour and exit at \(g(t)\) people/hour. Which condition means the number of people inside is decreasing?
  1. \(g(t)<0\)
  2. \(g'(t)<0\)
  3. \(f(t)-g(t)<0\)
  4. \(f'(t)-g'(t)<0\)
Answer and reasoning
C. If \(N(t)\) is the number inside, then \(N'(t)=f(t)-g(t)\). The building population decreases when \(N'(t)<0\).

Worked example: beach sand

Sand is removed at \(R(t)=1+6\sin\!\left(\frac{2\pi t}{25}\right)\) yd\(^3\)/hr and added at \(S(t)=\dfrac{18t}{1+4t}\) yd\(^3\)/hr.
\[ A'(6)=S(6)-R(6)\approx4.320-6.988=-2.668\ \text{yd}^3/\text{hr}. \]
At 6 hours, the total amount of sand on the beach is decreasing at approximately \(2.668\) cubic yards per hour.

Increasing or decreasing from a given derivative

The pollutant amount \(P(t)\) satisfies \[ P'(t)=2-4e^{-0.3\sqrt t}\quad\text{gallons/day}. \]
\(P'(7)=2-4e^{-0.3\sqrt7}\approx0.191>0\)

A cumulative-count model can reveal a problem

Suppose a cumulative shopper count is modeled by \[ E(t)=0.2t^4-10t^3+50t^2, \] for \(0\le t\le10\). Then \(E'(4)=-28.8\) shoppers/hour.
Context audit: a cumulative number who have entered cannot decrease. The derivative calculation is correct, but the model or claimed domain is not contextually valid near \(t=4\).
Calculus can test the plausibility of a model; do not force an impossible interpretation.

Seven common errors

  1. Omitting units.
  2. Using amount units for a derivative.
  3. Differentiating a rate when only net amount change is needed.
  4. Calling a negative derivative a “negative amount.”
  1. Using distant or same-side table values unnecessarily.
  2. Ignoring a domain or endpoint.
  3. Reporting an impossible contextual conclusion without questioning the model.

In economics, “marginal” means derivative: marginal cost is \(C'(x)\).

Fixed cost versus variable cost

A manufacturer has a monthly fixed cost of \$175,000 and a production cost of \$20 per unit.
\[ C(x)=175000+20x. \]

Fixed cost

\(C(0)=175000\): cost even when no units are produced.

Variable cost rate

\(C'(x)=20\): each additional unit adds exactly \$20 in this linear model.

Marginal cost is a local estimate

Definition

\[ MC=C'(x) \] It estimates the additional cost of producing one more unit near production level \(x\).

Estimate

Cost of the next unit \(\approx C'(x)\).

Exact difference

Cost of unit \(x+1\) is \(C(x+1)-C(x)\).

Marginal estimate versus exact next-unit cost

For \(C(x)=0.02x^2+2x+4000\), compare the marginal cost at \(x=100\) with the exact cost of the 101st unit.
\[ C'(x)=0.04x+2,\qquad C'(100)=6. \]
\[ C(101)-C(100)=6.02. \]
The derivative is a local linear estimate. It is close, but not necessarily exactly equal, to a one-unit finite difference.

Worked marginal-cost model

The cost of translating \(p\) pages is \[ C(p)=0.002p^3-0.1p^2+8.75p. \]

Marginal at 10 pages

\[ C'(10)=7.35\ \text{dollars/page}. \]

Exact 11th page

\[ C(11)-C(10)=7.312\ \text{dollars}. \]
Near 10 pages, total translation cost increases by about \$7.35 for one additional page.

Average cost is not marginal cost

Average cost

\[ \bar C(x)=\frac{C(x)}x. \] It spreads the total cost, including fixed cost, across all units.

For the model

\[ \bar C(100)=\frac{1500+5000+100}{100}=66 \] dollars/unit.
At \(x=100\), average cost is \$66 per unit while marginal cost is \$52 per additional unit.

Textbook extension: minimizing average cost

For \(C(x)=0.02x^2+2x+4000\), \[ \bar C(x)=0.02x+2+\frac{4000}{x}. \] Then \[ \bar C'(x)=0.02-\frac{4000}{x^2}. \]
\(\bar C'(x)=0\) gives \(x=\sqrt{200000}\approx447.2\). Thus the integer production level minimizing average cost is near 447 units.

Interpret marginal profit

If \(C'(400)=18\) and \(R'(400)=15\), then \[ \pi'(400)=15-18=-3\ \text{dollars/unit}. \]
Near 400 units, producing and selling one additional unit is expected to reduce profit by approximately \$3.
This is a marginal loss on the next unit, not necessarily a statement that total profit is negative.

Worked model: cost, revenue, and profit

For \(C(x)=1500+50x+0.01x^2\), suppose each unit sells for \$125.
\[ R(x)=125x, \] \[ \pi(x)=-0.01x^2+75x-1500. \]
\[ \pi'(x)=75-0.02x. \] At \(x=100\), \(\pi'(100)=73\) dollars/unit.
At 100 units, one additional unit is expected to increase profit by about \$73.

Maximum profit: use derivatives and the domain

For \(\pi(x)=-0.01x^2+75x-1500\), \[ \pi'(x)=75-0.02x=0\quad\Rightarrow\quad x=3750. \]

Derivative test

\(\pi'\) changes from positive to negative, so profit is maximized.

Maximum profit

\[ \pi(3750)=\$139{,}125. \]
If a feasible production interval is given, include its endpoints in the comparison.

Enrichment: elasticity of demand

If demand \(x=f(p)\) depends on price \(p\), the elasticity is \[ E(p)=\frac{p}{f(p)}f'(p). \]

Why unitless?

It compares relative change in demand with relative change in price.

Elastic demand

With this signed convention, demand is elastic when \(E<-1\).
Some economics texts define elasticity as \(-E\), making elastic demand correspond to a positive value greater than 1.

Elasticity predicts the direction of revenue

For \(f(p)=400(20-p)\), \[ E(p)=\frac{p}{p-20}. \] Demand is elastic when \(p>10\).
\[ R(p)=pf(p)=8000p-400p^2,\qquad R'(p)=8000-800p. \]
Revenue decreases exactly when \(p>10\), the same interval where \(E<-1\).

Chemical concentration and reaction rate

If \([C](t)\) is product concentration in moles/liter, then the instantaneous reaction rate is \[ \frac{d[C]}{dt}. \]

Positive derivative

Product concentration is increasing.

Derivative units

moles per liter per second, often written mol/(L·s).
A reaction-rate model may give \([C]'(t)\) directly rather than giving \([C](t)\).

Maximum rate in an autocatalytic reaction

Suppose \[ x'(t)=r x(t)[1-x(t)],\qquad r>0. \] Treat the rate as a function of concentration: \[ g(x)=rx(1-x). \] Then \(g'(x)=r(1-2x)\).
The reaction rate is greatest when \(x=\tfrac12\), exactly half the saturation concentration.

Generalized reaction-rate model

For \[ x'(t)=c x(t)[K-x(t)],\qquad c,K>0, \] find the concentration that maximizes the reaction rate.
\[ g(x)=cx(K-x),\qquad g'(x)=c(K-2x)=0\Rightarrow x=\frac K2. \]
The reaction proceeds most rapidly when the concentration reaches half of its limiting level \(K\).

Linear density is a derivative

If \(m(x)\) is the mass of the first \(x\) meters of a thin rod, then \[ \rho(x)=m'(x) \] is its linear mass density.
For \(m(x)=\sqrt{2x}\), \[ \rho(x)=\frac1{\sqrt{2x}}. \]
\[ \rho(2)=\frac12,\qquad \rho(8)=\frac14. \] The rod is less dense farther to the right.

Electric current is a rate of charge

Charge-current relationship

If \(Q(t)\) is electrical charge in coulombs, then \[ I(t)=Q'(t) \] is current in amperes, where 1 ampere = 1 coulomb/second.
Charge is an accumulated quantity; current is its instantaneous flow rate.

Differentiate a charge model

Suppose \[ Q(t)=10e^{-5t}+2te^{-2t}+3\sin(2t)-7\cos(2t) \] coulombs.
\[ I(t)=Q'(t)=-50e^{-5t}+2e^{-2t}-4te^{-2t}+6\cos(2t)+14\sin(2t). \]
The product rule is needed for \(2te^{-2t}\); current units are amperes.

Logistic population growth

A normalized population may satisfy \[ p'(t)=2p(t)[1-p(t)]. \] The growth rate as a function of population is \(g(p)=2p(1-p)\).
population \(p(t)\)growth rate \(p'(t)\)
Growth is fastest at \(p=\tfrac12\), the inflection point of the logistic population curve.

General logistic maximum-growth rule

For a logistic-type rate \[ P'(t)=rP(t)\left(1-\frac{P(t)}K\right), \] where \(K\) is carrying capacity, maximize the rate as a function of \(P\).
\[ g(P)=rP-\frac rK P^2,\qquad g'(P)=r-\frac{2r}{K}P=0\Rightarrow P=\frac K2. \]
A logistic population grows most rapidly at half its carrying capacity.

AP-style multiple choice I

A differentiable function \(R(t)\) gives the rate, in liters/minute, at which water enters a tank. What are the units of \(R'(8)\)?
  1. liters
  2. liters/minute
  3. liters/minute\(^2\)
  4. minutes/liter
Answer
C. The original function is already liters/minute, so differentiating with respect to minutes adds another denominator of minute.

AP-style multiple choice II

The number of bacteria is \(B(t)\). At \(t=6\), \(B'(6)>0\) and \(B''(6)<0\). Which statement is correct?
  1. The population is decreasing at an increasing rate.
  2. The population is increasing, but its growth rate is decreasing.
  3. The population is negative and decreasing.
  4. The growth rate is zero.
Answer
B. \(B'>0\) gives increasing population; \(B''<0\) says the growth rate \(B'\) is decreasing.

AP-style multiple choice III

A production rate is \(R(t)=4000+48(t-3)-4(t-3)^3\), where \(t\) is hours after 8 A.M. When is the production rate increasing most rapidly?
  1. 8:00 A.M.
  2. 10:00 A.M.
  3. 11:00 A.M.
  4. 1:00 P.M.
Answer
C. “Rate increasing most rapidly” asks for the maximum of \(R'(t)=48-12(t-3)^2\), which occurs at \(t=3\), or 11:00 A.M.

AP-style multiple choice IV

A company has \(C'(500)=24\) and \(R'(500)=29\). Which conclusion is best?
  1. Total profit is \$5.
  2. The 501st unit will increase profit by about \$5.
  3. The 501st unit costs exactly \$24.
  4. Profit is maximized at 500 units.
Answer
B. \(\pi'(500)=29-24=5\) dollars/unit. The derivative gives an approximation for one additional unit; it does not by itself prove a maximum.

AP free response: business model

A product has cost \(C(x)=900+28x+0.015x^2\) and sells for \$94 per unit, for \(0\le x\le3000\).
  1. Find \(C'(800)\) and interpret.
  2. Find \(\pi'(800)\) and interpret.
  1. Find the production level that maximizes profit.
  2. Justify with the derivative and domain.
Solution
\(C'=28+0.03x\), so \(C'(800)=52\) dollars/unit. \(\pi'=94-(28+0.03x)=66-0.03x\), so \(\pi'(800)=42\) dollars/unit. The critical point is \(x=2200\); \(\pi'\) changes from positive to negative and \(2200\) lies in the domain, so it maximizes profit.

Error analysis

Claim 1

“Because \(R'(12)<0\), the radioactive substance has a negative amount.”

Parameter challenge: infer a model

A chemical rate follows \(x'=cx(K-x)\). At one time, \(x=2\) and \(x'=3\); later, \(x=4\) and \(x'=4\). Find \(c\) and \(K\).
\[ 2c(K-2)=3,\qquad4c(K-4)=4. \]
Solution
From the equations, \(c(K-2)=1.5\) and \(c(K-4)=1\). Subtracting gives \(2c=0.5\), so \(c=0.25\). Then \(0.25(K-4)=1\), giving \(K=8\).

Very difficult: a rate of a net rate

Water enters a reservoir at \(I(t)=40+8\sin(t/2)\) L/min and leaves at \(O(t)=15+0.6t^2\) L/min.
  1. Find the net amount rate \(A'(t)\).
  2. Find \(A'(6)\) and interpret.
  1. Find \(A''(6)\).
  2. Explain how the amount can be increasing while its net rate is decreasing.
Solution
\(A'=25+8\sin(t/2)-0.6t^2\). \(A'(6)\approx4.529>0\) L/min, so the amount increases. \(A''=4\cos(t/2)-1.2t\), so \(A''(6)\approx-11.160\) L/min\(^2\). The net rate is positive but falling.

Ultra challenge: elasticity and marginal reasoning

Demand is \(x=f(p)=1200p^{-1/2}\), and unit production cost is modeled by \(C(x)=5000+4x+0.002x^2\).
  1. Find demand elasticity.
  2. Is demand elastic?
  3. Write revenue as a function of price.
  1. Write profit as a function of quantity by solving for price.
  2. Find a condition for maximum profit.
Outline
\(E=(p/f)f'=-1/2\), so demand is inelastic under the signed convention. From \(x=1200p^{-1/2}\), \(p=(1200/x)^2\), so \(R(x)=1{,}440{,}000/x\) and \(\pi(x)=1{,}440{,}000/x-5000-4x-0.002x^2\). A maximum candidate satisfies \(-1{,}440{,}000/x^2-4-0.004x=0\), which has no positive solution; profit decreases on \(x>0\), so the feasible-domain endpoint controls the optimum. This is a warning that the demand model may be unrealistic near small \(x\).

Exit ticket

A lake pollutant amount satisfies \(P'(7)=0.191\) gallons/day and \(P''(7)=-0.08\) gallons/day\(^2\). Write one sentence interpreting both values.
A company has \(MR=42\) dollars/unit and \(MC=47\) dollars/unit at \(x=900\). State what happens to profit near 900 units and whether this alone proves the total profit is negative.
Answers
At day 7, pollutant is increasing at 0.191 gal/day, while that rate is decreasing by 0.08 gal/day each day. Near 900 units, marginal profit is \(-5\) dollars/unit, so an additional unit lowers profit by about \$5; this does not prove total profit is negative.
