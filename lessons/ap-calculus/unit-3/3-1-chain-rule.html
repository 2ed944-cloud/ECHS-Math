The rule

Chain Rule

\[\frac{d}{dx}\big[f(g(x))\big]=f'(g(x))g'(x)\] \[\text{If }y=f(u)\text{ and }u=g(x),\quad \frac{dy}{dx}=\frac{dy}{du}\cdot\frac{du}{dx}.\]
In words: differentiate the outside while keeping the inside unchanged, then multiply by the derivative of the inside.
Do not confuse:
\[\frac{d}{dx}f(g(x))\neq f'(x)g'(x).\] The derivative of the outside is evaluated at \(g(x)\), not at \(x\).

Why the extra factor appears

Compare the slopes of: \[y=u^4,\qquad u=x^2+1.\] A small change in \(x\) first changes \(u\), then changes \(y\).
The total rate is: \[\text{rate of }y\text{ with respect to }u\times \text{rate of }u\text{ with respect to }x.\] \[\frac{dy}{dx}=4u^3(2x)=4(x^2+1)^3(2x).\]

Example 1: with and without the chain rule

Find \(f'(x)\) for: \[f(x)=(5x+3)^2.\] Method A: expand first.
Method B: use the chain rule.
Show guide
Expand: \(f(x)=25x^2+30x+9\), so \(f'(x)=50x+30\).

Chain rule: \(u=5x+3\), so \[f'(x)=2(5x+3)(5)=10(5x+3)=50x+30.\]

Example 2: power outside

Find \(f'(x)\): \[f(x)=(4-x^2)^3.\] Explain why the sign is easy to miss.
Show guide
Let \(u=4-x^2\). Then \(u'=-2x\). \[f'(x)=3(4-x^2)^2(-2x)=-6x(4-x^2)^2.\]

Logarithmic chain rule

\[\frac{d}{dx}\ln(g(x))=\frac{g'(x)}{g(x)},\qquad g(x)>0\]
Find the derivative and state the domain.
  1. \(f(x)=\ln(x^3-5x^5)\)
  2. \(g(x)=\ln(3^{5x})\)
  3. \(H(x)=\ln(\ln x)\)
Show guide
1. \(f'(x)=\dfrac{3x^2-25x^4}{x^3-5x^5}\), where \(x^3-5x^5>0\).
2. Since \(\ln(3^{5x})=5x\ln3\), \(g'(x)=5\ln3\).
3. \(H'(x)=\dfrac{1}{x\ln x}\), domain \(x>1\).

Domain notes that matter

Power notation can hide restrictions.
  • \(\sqrt{g(x)}\) requires \(g(x)\ge0\).
  • \(\ln(g(x))\) requires \(g(x)>0\).
  • \(\dfrac{1}{g(x)}\) requires \(g(x)\ne0\).
Derivative formulas may introduce new restrictions. Check the original function and the derivative expression.
  1. \(y=\sqrt{x^2-9}\)
  2. \(y=\sqrt[3]{x^2-9}\)
  3. \(y=\ln(x^2-9)\)
